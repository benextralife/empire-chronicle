# Silent Chess - Chapter 4: Rules in Red Ink

---

Fourth day. Dawn.

Zixuan walked to the courtyard carrying only her manual and the manual's silence.

The girl with the white ribbon was not there. In her place sat an old woman—eighty, perhaps ninety—wearing a faded indigo robe and a smile that had been worn smooth by decades. She placed a black stone at tengen.

Again. Always tengen.

Zixuan placed white at tengen.

The game moved differently this time. The old woman did not spiral outward. She built inward—tiny, precise enclosures that looked like nothing until suddenly each had become a wall, and Zixuan's stones found themselves surrounded, breathing in ever-tightening circles.

By midday, Zixuan had lost four groups.

"The manual never showed this pattern," she said, voice tight.

"You memorized the manual instead of understanding it." The old woman's voice was like dry leaves scraping stone. "A game of Go is not a sequence. It is a ecosystem. Every move changes the climate."

Zixuan stared at the board.

The coral-shaped formation the old woman had built—a Chinese opening abandoned by all serious players for two centuries—was not in the manual. The manual assumed its opponent would play classical moves.

But the academy did not play classical moves.

The academy played for keeps.

---

After the game, Zixuan did not go straight to her room.

She walked the corridors.

The servant was not following her today. Perhaps he had been ordered not to. Perhaps he had simply lost interest.

She passed doors bearing the twelve characters: **靜** (stillness), **虛** (emptiness), **明** (clarity), **捨** (letting go)… and the fourth door, sealed with red paper and silk cord. Behind it, she assumed, lay the things that losing players had been forced to leave.

A camera. A photograph of herself. What next?

She reached out and touched the red paper.

It was warm.

Not warm from sunlight. Warm like skin.

Zixuan pulled her hand back.

---

She found the red-lettered rules again, carved into a wooden beam above the corridor:

1. You may not speak to servants.
2. Once a stone is placed, it may not be moved.
3. After dusk, you must remain in your study.
4. If you hear your name called, do not answer.
5. The loser of every game must leave one thing behind.

She had read them a hundred times.

Today, she noticed something she had missed before. Beneath each rule, carved in smaller letters, was a second line—a translation into another script, older and cruder:

1. Do not trust your eyes.
2. Do not trust your memory.
3. Do not trust the dawn.
4. Do not trust silence.
5. Do not trust the thing you leave behind.

---

Zixuan returned to room seven with the manual open on her lap.

The rain had started again. It pattered against the paper wall in a rhythm that was almost a voice. She listened carefully.

The sound had patterns. Short-long-short. Long-short-short.

Morse-like. But not quite.

She traced the rhythm with her finger on the tatami.

**Dot-dot-dash. Dot-dash-dash.**

More than noise. A message.

Zixuan sat upright. Her heart beat faster.

The academy was not silent. The academy was speaking.

She only had to learn its language.
