# Silent Chess - Chapter 14: The Student Who Does Not Speak

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

Fourteenth day. Rain.

The academy had no weather, not really, but it remembered Kyoto's rain. The plum tree was dripping. The stone corridors were dark. The sound of water on tiles was the only clock the academy had ever needed.

Zixuan was teaching three students: Sakura, the monk, and the new one—the one who did not speak.

Margot had described her in a letter, delivered by a gear that materialized on the courtyard table one morning like a very polite pigeon: **She is twelve. She fixes watches. She does not speak because she has nothing to say that the gears have not already said better.**

Her name was Ai.

She arrived with a leather satchel full of disassembled watch movements, each no larger than a fingernail, each polished to a shine that caught the plum light and folded it into something brighter. She placed the satchel on the table, opened it, and took out a set of tweezers so fine they looked like silver thread.

She did not bow.
She did not smile.
She looked at the jade board and then at Zixuan and then at the board again, as though measuring the distance between intention and execution.

Zixuan placed a white stone at tengen.

Ai placed a black stone at tengen.

They played in silence.

By midday, Ai had surrounded twenty points of territory—not with the aggressive thickness Sakura favored, not with the fluid influence the monk preferred, but with **precision**. Every black stone was placed with the exactitude of a mainspring engaged into a gear train, each group independent, each connection timed to within a millimeter of tolerance.

Zixuan had never seen anything like it.

"You play like a watchmaker," she said.

Ai did not answer. She only placed another stone, this time at the third line, a shoulder hit so late, so calm, that it took Zixuan ten seconds to understand its purpose.

It was not an attack.
It was a **regulation**.

---

After the game, Ai opened her satchel again.

She took out a small, dismantled pocket watch—gold, enamel, the date window cracked, the balance spring missing. She placed it on the table beside the board.

"Can the academy fix this?" she asked.

Her voice was not silent. It was small. Roughed by disuse, like a hinge that had not been opened in a long time.

"The academy fixes what is broken," Zixuan said. "But the academy does not fix watches. It fixes attention."

Ai nodded. She understood.

---

They sat together in the rain.

Zixuan explained the game's history—the Tang monk, the dean, the manual, the red door, the girl with the white ribbon who was also herself, also Sachi, also the fifth wife, also the river. Ai listened, her hands never still, her tweezers moving over the watch parts as though they were stones on a board.

When Zixuan finished, Ai set down her tweezers.

"Will you teach me the third story?" she asked.

"The third story?"

"The one about the river that runs backward. The one my mother told me before she died."

Zixuan looked at her.

"You know the story?"

"I know the river. It runs through my city. It is why I fix watches. Because time leaks out of it, and someone has to put it back."

Zixuan reached into her bag and took out the sixth journal—the one that had been written by the girl with the white ribbon, the one that described the box that was not a box, the one that had ended with the words **I am the sixth**.

She handed it to Ai.

The girl's fingers brushed the leather. She closed her eyes.

"I have been waiting for this," she said.

---

That night, Zixuan sat under the plum tree and listened to the sound of Ai's tweezers—soft, rhythmic, metallic—working in the borrowed room the academy had provided.

Tick. Tick. Tick.

Not the watch.
The girl.
The girl was the watch. The girl was the river. The girl was the next stone at tengen.

Zixuan smiled into the dark.

The academy was not empty.
It had never been empty.
It had been **waiting**.

And now, finally, it was teaching.
