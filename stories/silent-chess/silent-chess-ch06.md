# Silent Chess - Chapter 6: Black and White

---

Sixth day. Before dawn.

Zixuan sat cross-legged on the tatami in room seven, the manual open beside her, pen in hand.

She had realized something during the night: the rain's Morse-like rhythm was not random. **Dot-dot-dash, dot-dash-dash.** Morse for the letter **S**, then **M**—S-M. Not a word. An initial.

Who was S.M.?

She had searched the manual. No initials. No signatures. Only a date, faintly visible under UV light: **咸豐五年**—the fifth year of the Xianfeng Emperor, 1855.

The manual's author had been alive in 1855.

And he had called himself a loser.

---

Zixuan stood at the red door before breakfast.

The photograph of herself at the archives lay on the stone in front of it. Beside it were the old woman's offering from the previous day—a pair of spectacles, thin-framed, with lenses cracked down the middle.

She had brought no gift herself.

The rules said the loser must leave one thing behind. Zixuan had not yet lost. She had no obligation.

But she had something she wanted to give.

From her pocket, she withdrew a single white Go stone—not the clamshell ones used in the academy, but one she had carried since childhood, a gift from her grandfather. Ivory, carved with a flower on the obverse. He had told her it was a master's first stone: the one you keep for the rest of your life, because it reminds you why you play.

She placed it on the stone before the door.

"I'm not losing this," she whispered. "I'm leaving it as a promise."

No wind answered. No crack of thunder. Only the distant plum tree, dripping silently.

---

The morning game began with a new opponent—a boy of no more than twelve, wearing a grey scholar's robe identical to the first young man's. He knelt across from Zixuan and placed a black stone at tengen.

Tengen again. Always tengen.

Zixuan placed white at tengen.

This time, something shifted.

The moment her fingers left the stone, she felt it—a faint current moving through the jade board, like water beneath ice. The board was not stone. It was something else.

Her opponent's eyes flicked upward, surprised.

"You felt it," he said.

"What is this place?"

He did not answer. Instead, he played a move so strange—a stone placed halfway between two intersection points, off-grid, in empty air—that Zixuan stared, then laughed, then stopped laughing because the stone remained hovering in defiance of every rule of physics and Go she knew.

"Where is that?"

"Nowhere." The boy smiled. "And everywhere. This is what happens when you stop playing the board and start playing the room."

Zixuan's breath caught.

She looked at her manual. The handwriting on page 87 seemed to tremble, as though the ink were still wet.

**The real game is played in the corridors, between moves, behind the red chamber.**

She had read it twice. Had never understood it.

Now, watching a stone float in midair, she began to.

---

The game ended at noon—not in checkmate, not in territory collapse, but in a silence so complete that Zixuan could hear her own heart beating in the hollow spaces of her skull.

Her opponent had surrounded nothing. Zixuan had surrounded nothing. The board was empty, except for one floating black stone and two white stones at tengen and D4.

"You did not lose today," the boy said, standing up. "But you did not win, either. There is a third option."

"What is it?"

"To ask the question the game has been waiting for you to ask."

"What question?"

He did not answer. He turned and walked toward the corridor, his footsteps echoing on the stone. Before he turned the corner, he looked back.

"Find the stone that does not match the board," he said. "And then ask it who put it there."

---

Zixuan spent the afternoon looking for the wrong stone.

The courtyard's jade board was perfect—sixty-four intersection lines, none broken, none duplicated. The stones in the ceramic jars were flawless—three hundred and sixty black, three hundred and sixty white, identical in weight and texture.

Corridor. Room seven. Red door.

Inside her bag, the manual felt heavier than before.

She sat beneath the plum tree and opened it. The water damage on page 87 was not accidental. Under magnification, it would have formed a shape—an opening diagram.

She carried no magnifying glass.

But she had her camera. And the chain of thought she had followed, step by step, for three years.

Zixuan photographed page 87 from directly above, then tilted the image ninety degrees. The water stain became a grid—an invisible grid sketched onto a board that was not there.

A hidden layer.

She photographed the other pages. Several carried the same mark: faint diagonal lines, invisible unless the page was photographed at an angle.

This was the manual's real score—not the one she had memorized, but a second game embedded in the first. A game written in light and shadow, for an opponent who could see what she could not.

She looked up.

The red door stood twenty paces away. The photograph of herself and her grandfather's ivory stone lay before it.

In her bag, the manual whispered with her breath.

Zixuan stood up.

It was time to ask the door a question.
