# Silent Chess - Chapter 13: The Clock Restorer Arrives

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

Thirteenth day. Morning.

She was forty-two, as the girl had predicted. Her name was Margot Hale. She had come from a city of gears and rust and winter—Cleveland, though she did not know that word yet—carrying a leather satchel full of broken watches and a silence that was not empty but **waiting**.

Zixuan found her sitting on the torii steps, staring at the garden as though trying to remember a dream.

"You came," Zixuan said.

"I followed the ticking." Margot looked up. Her eyes were grey, practical, the kind that measured distance before stepping. "I have not played Go since my father taught me when I was twelve. He said I had a talent for sacrifice. I said I hated losing. We never played again."

"We do not lose here," Zixuan said. "We return."

Margot smiled—small, cautious, the smile of someone who has been hurt by promises before. "Everyone loses something. Even stories."

"Yes." Zixuan stepped aside, gesturing to the open gate. "Come in. The board is ready."

---

Margot crossed the courtyard with the unhurried certainty of a woman who fixes things for a living—who knows that every mechanism has a center, that every silence has a rhythm, that the trick is not forcing the parts to cooperate but learning the language they have been speaking all along.

She stopped at the jade board.

She did not kneel. She placed her satchel on the stone table and opened it, revealing not watches but **gears**—brass and steel, of every size, some the size of a thumbnail, some the size of a dinner plate, each polished to a shine that held the plum tree's reflection like a secret.

"I brought these," she said.

"Why?"

"Because a clock is just a board that moves." She lifted a small gear and placed it on the intersection at tengen. "And a board is just a clock that remembers."

Zixuan looked at the gear. It was not a stone. It was not the right shape. It would not stay where it was placed.

But when she set her own ivory stone beside it, the gear **locked** into place.

They played for six hours.

It was not a game of territory. It was a game of **rhythm**. Margot placed her gears at intersections that corresponded to escapement points—anchor, escape wheel, balance spring. Zixuan responded with stones that marked beats—quarter notes, half notes, whole notes. By midday, the board looked less like a Go game and more like the diagram of a movement from a symphony neither of them had heard but both recognized.

At dusk, Margot sat back.

"I have to go home," she said. "My patients are waiting."

"Patients?"

"The clocks. The ones that have forgotten how to tell time." She packed her gears with the same unhurried care she had shown in opening them. "But I will come back. This"—she nodded at the board—"is the most complicated mechanism I have ever touched."

"It is not a mechanism," Zixuan said.

"What is it?"

"A memory," Zixuan said. "A very large one. And it has learned to tick."

---

Margot crossed the torii at sunset.

Zixuan watched her go. The girl with the white ribbon appeared at her side, silent as a shadow.

"She is the seventh," the girl said.

"The last?"

"The next. The academy does not count in sevens. It counts in **returns**."

Zixuan looked down at the board. The gears were still there, locked into their intersections, picking up the plum tree's light and turning it into something that looked like time.

She reached out and touched the largest gear—the one Margot had placed at tengen.

It was warm.

And it was ticking.

---

Zixuan sat under the plum tree that night and listened.

The academy was no longer a place. It was a frequency—carried in the gears, in the stones, in the inkstone, in the stories, in the silent space between one breath and the next. It would keep returning, patient as gravity, to whoever needed it.

Her grandfather had been one.
The girl had been one.
Evelyn had been one.
Margot would be one.

And somewhere, in a city of clocks that had forgotten how to tell time, the next player was already reaching for a tool, already hearing the tick of a mechanism that was not quite a mechanism, already beginning to translate the silence between beats into a language only she could hear.

Zixuan smiled.

The board was empty.
The garden was quiet.
The plum tree dropped a single petal, white on black stone, like a note at the end of a long song.

She placed a stone at tengen—not for this game, but for the next one.

The stone remained.

Because the game always remains.

And the first move is always an act of faith.
