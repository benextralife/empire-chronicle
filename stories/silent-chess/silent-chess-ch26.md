# Silent Chess - Chapter 26: The Boy Who Counted Nothing

---

Twenty-sixth day.

She walked for six hours before she saw the boy.

He was sitting on a stone wall at the side of the road, swinging his legs and counting something that was not there. His mouth moved. His fingers moved. His eyebrows moved in the precise rhythm of arithmetic. He did not look up when she approached, which was either rudeness or concentration so deep it had no room for anything else.

"Good morning," Zixuan said.

The boy kept counting.

"What are you counting?" she asked.

"Nothing," he said.

"How can you count nothing?"

"You count the spaces where things are not," he said, as if this were obvious. "Zero. One. Two. Three. All the numbers are just ways of measuring how much is missing."

Zixuan stopped. She looked at the road. She looked at the fields on either side, yellow with early harvest, the houses scattered like dice thrown by a careless hand, the clouds moving slowly enough to prove that time was not a straight line but a series of glances the sky takes at itself.

"You are wrong," she said.

The boy stopped counting. He looked at her for the first time. He was perhaps eleven. He had dirt on his face and shoes with holes in the toes and an expression that had not yet learned to be polite.

"I am never wrong," he said.

"Numbers are not about missing things. They are about having things. One apple, two apples, three apples."

The boy swung his legs again. He counted for a moment in silence — one, two, three — then looked at her with the patience of a mountain addressing a stream.

"That is not counting," he said. "That is naming."

Zixuan sat down on the wall beside him. The stone was warm. The road was empty. The sound of the wind moving through the uncut grain was the same as the sound of gears turning in a room with no walls, and she realized she had been hearing it for days without knowing what it was.

"My name is Zixuan," she said.

"Numbers do not need names," the boy said. "But I am called Ren."

"Why do you count nothing, Ren?"

"Because everything else is already counted," he said. "The sheep. The coins. The days until the festival. No one counts the spaces. If you do not count the spaces, you do not know how much room you have left."

"Room for what?"

"For what comes next."

Zixuan thought about this. She thought about the academy without walls. She thought about the students who had learned to listen to the board instead of touching it. She thought about the gears beneath her feet, which had been walls and floors and ceilings and had then become something else — a surface, a medium, a way of holding still while everything changed.

"I have been teaching people to play chess," she said.

"Good," Ren said. "Chess is the best game for counting nothing. Every square is either empty or it is not. Every move opens a space and closes one. You are always counting what is missing."

"Were you there?" she asked. "In the academy?"

"I was never anywhere," Ren said. "I am here."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the only answer there is." He slid off the wall and landed lightly in the dust. "Come. I will show you the field where the sheep are not."

Zixuan followed him.

They walked for two miles through fields that smelled of late summer and machinery that had already been put away for the season. The boy spoke as they walked, not continuously but in bursts, like a stream that rises and falls with the lay of the land.

"My mother counts threads," he said. "My father counts coins. My sister counts the ways she is unhappy. I count nothing so that when something happens, I have room for it."

"Does something happen often?"

"All the time," Ren said. "You just saw it happen when you sat on the wall. Something new just happened when you asked me about chess. The sky just happened when it turned that color blue."

Zixuan looked up. The sky was the color of a cat's eye, greenish at the horizon, deepening toward the top. It was the color of the room of clocks at a certain hour she could not name, the hour between the last bell and the first, when all the clocks in the world are lying in unison.

They reached a field on a hill. There were no sheep. There was no fence. There was only grass, tall and dry and whispering in the wind, and a single oak tree at the center with a rope hanging from one low branch.

"This is the field," Ren said.

"It is empty."

"Yes."

Zixuan sat down in the grass. Ren sat beside her. They watched the sky change color for an hour without speaking, which was not an hour of silence but an hour of listening — to the grass, to the wind, to the sound of nothing being counted and counted and counted again until it became something that was not nothing anymore.

"You should teach me," Zixuan said.

"Teach you what?"

"To count nothing properly."

Ren smiled. He was eleven years old, and his smile had the gravity of someone who had been waiting a long time for someone to ask the right question.

"I have been waiting for a student," he said.
