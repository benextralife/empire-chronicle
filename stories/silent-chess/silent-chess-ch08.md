# Silent Chess - Chapter 8: The Monk's Staff

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

Zixuan held the iron staff.

It was heavier than it looked, and colder. Eight bronze rings chimed faintly when she lifted it, like distant bells calling across a valley. The servant had set it down before the red door with the quiet finality of a verdict.

She had expected to lose something of her own.

Instead, the Tang monk had lost nothing.

That meant he had won.

But the manual—the manual she had memorized, worshipped, pinned to her desk for three years—described a losing game. Every annotation, every marginal tragedy, every ghostly instruction in cramped ink had belonged to a man who had written his own defeat. Three hundred years ago, a man had sat in this courtyard, played this board, and left behind a score that invited another player to come and lose.

Because that was the game's nature. It required sacrifice.

So why had the monk won?

Zixuan returned to the courtyard at dawn. The girl with the white ribbon was waiting.

"He didn't lose," the girl said, answering a question Zixuan had not asked aloud. "He renounced."

"Renounced what?"

"His victory. His progress. His whole identity as a player." She placed a black stone at tengen, as ritual demanded. "The game feeds on wanting. The only way to escape it is to stop wanting to win or lose—and just play."

Zixuan sat. She placed white at tengen.

They played in silence.

By midday, Zixuan's territory was not collapsing. It was widening—not because she was attacking, but because she was **letting go**. Each move was clean, modest, unhungry. The girl's counterattacks brushed against her like wind against a wall—visible, harmless.

"You understand now," the girl said.

"I don't want to win," Zixuan said quietly. "I just want to find out what this place is."

"That is the same thing."

---

At dusk, Zixuan set down her last stone.

The girl surveyed the board. Then she smiled—full, warm, the smile of someone who had been waiting for permission to rest.

"You have my leave," she said. "The dean is in the tower at the back. Take the staff. It will recognize him."

Zixuan looked at the iron rod in her hands.

"What happens when I meet him?"

"Everything you remember changes."

---

She walked through the rear corridor, past the sealed rooms, past the plum tree still dripping after the morning's rain. The staff grew lighter in her hands, almost weightless, as if it were eager to reach its owner.

The tower stood at the academy's edge—a round, three-story structure of pine timber, its upper floor open to the sky. A single lamp burned inside.

Xuanzang was waiting for her.

"Monk?" Zixuan said.

He smiled—his face warm, unhurried, unchanged. "Restorer. I wondered when you would come. The manual led you sooner than I expected."

"That was you?"

"I wrote a version of it. The version you found. The score that brought you here."

"But—why? Why invite losers into this place?"

"Because losers are the only ones ready to see what the game really is." He gestured to the floor, where the jade board from the courtyard now rested, floating inches above the tatami. "Sit. I will show you."

---

They sat facing each other across the floating board.

Xuanzang placed a black stone at tengen.

"You think this is a story about Go," he said. "It is not. It is a story about **attention**. Every move you make here is an act of attention. What you place on the board, you also place in your life. What you abandon at the red door, you abandon in your memory. The academy is a lens—magnifying what you refuse to see."

Zixuan looked down.

The board was no longer a board. The intersection lines had become streets, walls, corridors. The stones were doors. The single black stone at tengen was a figure, standing at the horizon of an impossible city.

"You have been inside this game before," Xuanzang said. "Not this version. A previous one. When you were younger. When you first learned Go."

---

Zixuan remembered.

A summer afternoon. Her grandfather's hands on hers. The scratch of slate on wood. The weight of a hand-carved white stone.

"The game your grandfather taught you," Xuanzang said, "was not a game. It was an inheritance. He was one of us—a watcher, a player who chose to step outside the academy's corridors and teach the real language."

"But he never told me."

"He could not. The rules forbid it."

Xuanzang leaned forward. "The academy chose you. Not because you are a restorer. Not because you found the manual. Because your blood remembers the board."

Zixuan felt tears on her cheeks.

She did not remember crying.
