# Silent Chess - Chapter 5: The Player Who Watches

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

Fifth day. Noon.

Zixuan stood in the courtyard and listened to the rain.

The rhythm was the same as yesterday: **dot-dot-dash, dot-dash-dash**. She had written it on her wrist with a ballpoint pen, but the ink ran in the damp air. She memorized it instead, repeating it like a sutra.

She had not slept.

The manual lay open to page 87. The marginalia, which she had once treated as decoration, now seemed to pulse with meaning. The cramped handwriting belonged to the opponent who had said he wrote it three hundred years ago. He had left clues—not in the moves, but in the annotations.

> Tengen is not an opening. It is a door.

> The game is not played on the board. The board is only the scorecard.

> The real game is played in the corridors, between moves, behind the red door.

Zixuan looked up.

The servant was watching her from the archway. He had not moved in two days.

"Tell me," she said. "Who built this academy?"

"The one who won."

"And the one who lost?"

"The one who built it, too."

---

That afternoon, she played a third opponent.

He was a man in his forties, wearing a dark green robe, with a scar across his left eyebrow. He opened at tengen again.

Zixuan opened white at tengen as well.

The game progressed with a discipline she had never encountered. Every black move was tight, economical, exact. There were no tricks, no spirals, no unorthodox experiments. Just pure, cold efficiency.

By sunset, she had lost by one point.

"One point," she said, staring at the board. "One point."

"You were better than yesterday," the man said. He gathered the stones without looking at her. "But you were still playing the manual."

"What should I play?"

"The manual was written by a man who wanted to lose." He paused at the archway. "Think about that."

---

Zixuan returned to her room and lit an oil lamp.

She thought about the manual's author. He had described a game that lasted three days. He had described his own defeat. He had left the score as an invitation.

A man who wanted to lose.

Not a master recording a victory. A loser leaving breadcrumbs.

She turned to the final page again and this time read the marginalia in full:

> I write this so that someone will come.
> I cannot finish the game. I have already left too much behind.
> But if you are reading this, you are in the room where I died.
> Find the stone that does not match the board.
> Find the door that does not open inward.
> Find the name that is written on the wall behind the red chamber.
> That is where the real game begins.

Zixuan closed the book.

She had spent three years restoring Ming scrolls. She knew what to look for.

She stood up and blew out the lamp.

In the dark, she could hear the rain whispering: **dot-dot-dash, dot-dash-dash**—the same pattern, over and over, patient as a heartbeat.

Tonight, she would not sleep.

Tonight, she would find the wrong stone.
