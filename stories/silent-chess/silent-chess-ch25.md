# Silent Chess - Chapter 25: The Academy That Had No Walls

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。

Twenty-fifth day.

The girl woke and the sound was gone.

She stood, and the gears beneath her feet did not stop turning. They had become the floor, the ceiling, the walls. The corridor had dissolved into a single great hall without doors, without windows, without corners. It was a room the size of a city, and in it were all the students she had ever taught, sitting in concentric circles around a chessboard made of light.

Zixuan walked to the center.

The board was empty. Not because no pieces had been placed. Because the pieces had grown tired of form.

"You are late," said a student she did not recognize.

"I am not late," she said. "I am exactly on time."

She sat cross-legged on the gears. The students watched. The light-board waited. Somewhere beyond the ceiling that was not a ceiling, a bell rang — not a clock bell, not a school bell, but the kind of bell that rings only when something ends and something else begins simultaneously.

The first student to speak was the one who had smiled on the twenty-fourth day.

"What do we play for now?" he asked.

Zixuan looked at the empty board. She looked at the circle of faces. She looked at the gears turning beneath her knees, carrying the weight of the building and the memory of walls and the scent of plum trees that no longer grew anywhere in the world.

"We play," she said, "for the joy of the next move."

The student hesitated. "That is not a prize."

"Then you have been playing for the wrong reasons," she said, and placed her hand on the center of the board. Where her palm touched the light, a white pawn appeared. It did not glitter. It did not gleam. It simply was.

The student placed a black pawn opposite it.

The game began.

It was not like any game Zixuan had ever seen. The pieces moved before either player touched them. The board shifted its own squares. The score of the game was written in the air between them, visible only if you stopped trying to see it and started trying to listen.

After three hours, the student smiled again.

"Now I understand," he said.

"Understand what?" Zixuan asked.

"Strategy," he said. "Strategy is not about winning. It is about staying alive long enough to keep playing."

Zixuan laughed. The sound was strange to her own ears — it had been so long since she had laughed that she had forgotten laughter had a weight, a temperature, a shape.

The gears beneath them pulsed once, twice, then fell silent.

The hall had walls again. The windows showed a garden. The plum tree was back, and it was blooming, and the blossoms were falling into the center of the chessboard like slow white snow.

Zixuan stood. The students stood. They did not applaud. They did not thank her. They simply watched her walk toward the door that had reappeared at the far end of the hall.

She did not look back.

Outside, the sky was gray and vast. The road stretched in both directions. The bag on her shoulder was lighter than it had been when she arrived, and heavier than it would be when she left, because what she carried now was not what she had learned but what she was about to unlearn.

The girl began to walk.

Twenty-fifth day.

She left the academy behind.
