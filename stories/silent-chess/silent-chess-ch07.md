# Silent Chess - Chapter 7: The Saint's Victory

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

The Tang monk stepped into the room with the air of a man who had already lost.

Xuanzang—call him Tang Sanzang, as the storybooks did—carried nothing but a staff and an appetite for scripture that could move mountains. He had walked from Chang'an to Tianzhu and back again, surviving demons with appetite and kings with worse intentions. He had learned to negotiate with ghosts.

The room in front of him did not have ghosts. It had a board. A score. A single black stone already placed at the star point (Q4), as if waiting for a conversation that had been delayed for centuries.

"You play black," said the servant in the blue robe, who had not moved from the doorway in three days.

"I have no skill at this game," Xuanzang said.

"Good. The game does not reward skill."

The servant withdrew. The door closed.

Xuanzang sat. He placed a white stone at the shoulder attack (D4)—just as the manual had described, in faded ink on a leaf inside the sutra bag.

The opponent across from him was not a ghost, not a demon, not a monk. It was a **woman** in grey scholar's robes, with a face that had been worn smooth by patience and a smile that knew the shape of every possible move before the stone was lifted.

"I know you," she said.

"I do not know you," Xuanzang replied.

"You will. You are here to win, aren't you?"

"I am here to go home."

"Then lose," she said. "The game always takes. That is its purpose."

---

The game lasted one hundred and ninety-three moves.

It was not a competition. Each stone Xuanzang placed—each humble classical response, each careful extension—was answered not with brilliance but with **mirroring**. His opponent played the tone, not the theme. Where he built territory, she built territory. Where he created influence, she created influence. By move seventy, the board was so balanced that it looked like a photograph of silence.

Xuanzang knew he had to break it.

He found the move in the **Diamond Sutra**, ironically the one scripture he had been translating when the imperial messenger arrived. A line about form being emptiness, emptiness being form. He translated it into Go: sacrifice the center to save the edge.

It was a **soul's sacrifice**. In Go terms, it was madness.

His opponent tilted her head. "Sacrifice?"

"Wisdom," he said.

She studied the board. Studied him. Then, with the faintest exhalation, she placed a black stone where the manual had warned him against playing—the spot that would cost him the game but give him the outcome he actually wanted.

Xuanzang placed white.

He had lost his territory. But he had won the corridor.

---

"I leave my staff," he said.

The servant came forward. He reached for the monk's iron staff—eight rings, legendary weight, the kind that could subdue a river dragon. The servant lifted it without effort.

"No," said the woman. "He loses nothing. He wins everything."

The servant paused. Looked at her. Then set the staff down and walked toward the red door without—without—saying a word.

Xuanzang remained seated. His hands were empty. His mind was not.

"Who are you?" he asked her.

"I am the game," she said. "I am every game. Every game you have ever played, and will ever play."

"And the red door?"

"Is the game you are playing now. Go through it. The dean is waiting."

Xuanzang stood. He felt light, as though the journey from Chang'an had finally ended.

He crossed the courtyard. The plum tree bloomed out of season. The jade board gleamed like an open eye.

At the red door, he turned.

"Will I see you again?" he asked.

"Every time you play," she said. "Every time you surrender. Every time you choose the harder thing."

Xuanzang opened the door.

---

In **Silent Chess**, the real opponent is not the person across from you.

The real opponent is the game itself—the ancient, hungry thing that feeds on stories, on memory, on the stubborn human belief that a board can contain a soul.

The Tang monk walked through his door. His chapter ends here, with a staff left behind and a heart still traveling.

In the next chapter, another player will sit. Another stone will fall. The game will continue, because the game always continues.

That is its nature. That is its curse.

And somewhere, in a corridor that smells of plum and rain, a woman in a grey robe will smile and wait for the next person brave enough—or foolish enough—to pick up a stone and begin.
