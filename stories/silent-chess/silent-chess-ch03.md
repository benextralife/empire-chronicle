# Silent Chess - Chapter 3: First Game

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

The morning came grey and cold.

Zixuan woke to the sound of water dripping in the courtyard. The servant was already outside her door, holding a tray with a bowl of rice gruel and a single pickled plum.

"Eat," he said. "The game runs from dawn to dusk. No breaks."

She ate quickly, washing the gruel down with bitter tea. Her hands still shook. The previous day's collapse—her lower-left territory crumbling under black's merciless shoulder attack—felt like a bruise on her mind.

You do not play with your hands, she reminded herself. You play with your fear.

She walked to the courtyard at sunrise.

The jade board steamed in the cool air. Her opponent from yesterday had not yet appeared. Instead, a new figure sat across from the empty seat—a girl of no more than sixteen, wearing a faded pink robe, her hair braided with a single white ribbon.

"You play today," the servant said. "He does not play two days in a row."

"Who are you?" Zixuan asked the girl.

She did not answer. She only reached for a black stone and placed it at the center tengen.

---

The center.

Zixuan had never seen anyone open at tengen in a serious game. It was a child's move—provincial, arrogant. In the ancient manual, the first move had been at Q4, the star point, humble and classical.

The girl watched her with round, dark eyes. Her expression was not mocking. It was simply… waiting.

Zixuan's fingers closed around a white stone.

The manual had said:

> Black responds with a shoulder attack at D4.

But the manual had also described a second opening—a variant that only appeared if white refused to play at the star point. The marginalia were faint, almost erased by time:

> If white chooses tengen, black must occupy the center before the corners are settled.

Zixuan placed her first white stone at tengen as well.

The girl smiled for the first time. A small, secret smile, like a cat that has found an open window.

---

The game became a spiral.

White at tengen. Black at tengen. Then black at D4. Then white at D16—a bold, impossible extension that no restrained master would ever dare. The girl's stones flowed outward in widening circles, not building territory but projecting force, like ripples from a stone dropped into a pond.

Zixuan tried to hold the center. She tried to contain the girl's influence. But every response felt two moves too slow.

By mid-afternoon, she had lost three groups.

By evening, she had lost the board.

"The game lasts three days," her opponent said, standing up. "But you have already lost today. That means—"

"—I must leave something behind," Zixuan said.

The girl nodded. She reached into her sleeve and pulled out a small, folded piece of paper. She placed it on the board.

Zixuan unfolded it.

It was a photograph of herself—taken at the archives, three years ago, standing in front of a shelf of Ming scrolls. The girl could not possibly have this image.

"Where did you get this?"

The girl only smiled, turned, and walked away.

---

Zixuan handed the photograph to the servant. He carried it to the fourth door—the red-sealed chamber—and returned with a fresh knot tied to the cord.

"Tomorrow," he said, "your opponent will be different. The same rule applies. Leave one thing. Lose it if you lose the game."

"Does anyone ever win?" Zixuan asked.

The servant's face showed nothing. "That is not the question."

---

She returned to room seven.

The rain had stopped. Moonlight poured through the paper window, bleaching the tatami to silver. Zixuan sat cross-legged on the floor and opened her score manual, page 87.

She had memorized the moves, the variations, the hidden life-and-death problems. But the manual had not prepared her for a tengen opening. It had not prepared her for a girl who smiled with a photograph of herself in her sleeve.

She turned to the final page of the manual. There, in the cramped marginalia she had noticed a hundred times but never truly read:

> **This game is not played with stones.**
> **This game is played with memory.**
> **The one who remembers wins.**
> **The one who forgets leaves.**

Zixuan closed the book.

Outside, the plum tree rustled. Somewhere deep in the academy, a bell tolled once.

Tonight, she would not sleep.

Tonight, she would relearn the manual—not as a sequence of moves, but as a living map of the game's secret grammar.

Tomorrow, she would not play with her hands.

She would play with her memory.
