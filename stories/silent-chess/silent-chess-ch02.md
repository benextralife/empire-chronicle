# Silent Chess - Chapter 2: The Rain Pavilion

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

The courtyard smelled of wet stone and plum blossoms.

Zixuan set her bag down beside the jade board. The stone was cool under her palm, polished by three hundred years of hands. Black stones, carved from obsidian, filled a ceramic jar on the left. White, from clamshell, on the right.

No clock. No calendar. Only the rain and the plum tree.

"You play white," the servant said. He had not moved from the archway. "Your opponent arrives at dusk."

"Who is my opponent?"

"The man who wins."

---

She spent the afternoon studying the courtyard.

A corridor wrapped around three sides of the space, opening onto twelve identical rooms. Each door bore a paper lantern with a single Chinese character: **靜**, **虛**, **明**, **捨**… twelve states of mind that Go masters were supposed to cultivate.

The fourth door was sealed with red paper and silk cord.

Zixuan raised her camera again.

"Don't," the servant warned. "Some things cannot be photographed."

"Rules?"

"The academy's rules." He leaned on his cane. "You have agreed to them. That makes them real enough."

---

Opponent arrived with the twilight.

He was young—no older than twenty-five—wearing a grey scholar's robe. His hair was tied in a simple knot. He carried no bag, no stones, no fan. Only a jade cup, which he placed on the board's edge before sitting.

"You are the restorer," he said.

"You know me?"

"I know the manual." He nodded at her bag. "You found it in Tianjin, inside a book of Li Shangyin's poetry. Page 87."

Zixuan stiffened. "Did you put it there?"

"I wrote it," he said. "Three hundred years ago."

---

The first move was white, at star point (Q4).

The stranger placed a black stone at D4—a shoulder attack, exactly as the manual described.

Zixuan's hand trembled.

She tried to remember the rest of the game. She had spent fifteen years memorizing this score. Every fight, every sacrifice, every hidden ko. But memory was not the same as living it.

By move seventy, she was already lost.

By move one hundred and twelve, her entire lower-left territory had collapsed.

"The game lasts three days," her opponent said, sipping from his jade cup. "You do not have to finish today."

"I have to."

"Then you are already bankrupt." He placed a black stone at the center of the board. "And now, you must leave something behind."

---

Zixuan looked down at her own hands.

The servant stepped forward. "The rule is simple. You leave what matters most to you. The academy keeps it until the game ends. If you win, you get it back. If you lose—"

"—it stays," Zixuan finished.

"Yes."

She reached into her pocket and pulled out the photograph she had taken of the jade board. She set it on the table, face down.

"This is my camera."

The servant nodded, picked up the photograph, and carried it to the fourth door—the one sealed with red paper and silk cord. He placed the photograph before the door, tied a fresh knot in the cord, and stepped back.

"Tomorrow," he said, "you will begin at dawn."

---

She slept in room number seven: **虛** (emptiness).

The floor was tatami. The walls were paper. A single window looked out onto the rain-dark courtyard, where the plum tree dripped and the jade board gleamed faintly in the moonlight.

Zixuan lay awake for a long time, listening to the rain.

She replayed the game in her head. Every mistake. Every missed opportunity. The shoulder attack at D4 should have been a pincer jump. She had known that. The ancient manual had said so, in the cramped marginalia she had copied a hundred times.

But knowing and doing were different.

This was what the game wanted from her. Not memory, not technique—surrender.

Only then could she truly see.

Outside, the wind rose. The plum tree shivered. Somewhere in the academy's corridors, a wooden door creaked open and shut.

Zixuan closed her eyes.

Tomorrow, she would not play with her hands.

She would play with her fear.
