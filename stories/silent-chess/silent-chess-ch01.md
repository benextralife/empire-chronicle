# Silent Chess - Chapter 1: The Ancient Score

---

The score arrived on a Tuesday, tucked inside a book of Ming dynasty poetry.

Lin Zixuan had been restoring scrolls at the Tianjin Municipal Archives for three years when she first encountered the notation. It was not poetry at all. The marginalia, cramped and urgent, described a game of Go played on a nineteen-by-nineteen board that did not exist anywhere in the archives' collection.

She traced the characters with a gloved finger.

- White opens at star point (Q4).
- Black responds with a shoulder attack at D4.
- The game lasted three days.
- Only one player survived.

The final line promised a location: **Rain Pavilion Academy, Yandang Mountains**.

That night, Zixuan dreamt of a vermilion door that would not close.

---

She left before dawn.

The Yandang range rose out of Zhejiang like a labyrinth of granite. Rain fell in sheets, turning the hiking path into mud. By noon, she had lost the trail twice. By dusk, she found it again—a stone archway half-buried in moss, carved with four characters:

**聽雨閣 · 觀棋生死**

The rain pavilion. Where watching a game meant wagering your life.

Zixuan raised her camera. The lens fogged. When she wiped it clear, a figure stood at the archway's edge—an old man in a blue cotton robe, holding a bamboo cane.

"You shouldn't have come," he said.

"Are you the keeper?"

"I am a servant." He gestured beyond the arch. "The master is waiting. But you must understand the rules before you step inside."

He pointed to the red-lettered inscriptions on either side of the gate:

1. You may not speak to servants.
2. Once a stone is placed, it may not be moved.
3. After dusk, you must remain in your study.
4. If you hear your name called, do not answer.
5. The loser of every game must leave **one thing** behind.

Zixuan took a photograph. "Are these genuine Ming prohibitions?"

The servant smiled without warmth. "They are the academy's terms of survival. As for your question—genuine or not, you will abide by them."

He stepped aside.

The gate swung inward with a groan of iron hinges. Beyond it lay a courtyard paved with black stone, and at its center, under a plum tree in full bloom, stood a Go board carved from a single slab of jade.

A single white stone had already been played—at (Q4), the star point.

The game had begun.
