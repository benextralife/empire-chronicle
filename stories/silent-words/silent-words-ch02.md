# The Silence Between Words - Chapter 2: The First Door

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

The interior of the box smelled of camphor and iron.

Evelyn lifted the lid. Beneath it lay seven layers of silk wrapping, each dyed a different color—indigo, rust, gold, cream, black, grey, white. The seventh layer, white, was stitched with a single Chinese character in silver thread: **譯**.

Translate.

She unfolded the silk. Inside were seven journals, bound in deteriorating leather, each about the size of a passport. The pages were brittle, the ink faded to the color of dried blood. The handwriting was a woman's—small, slanted, meticulous, with a habit of underlining certain syllables as though they were prayers.

The first journal had no title page. Only a date, written in English at the top of the first leaf:

**October 17, 1956. Kyoto.**

> I have begun.
>
> The old woman at the temple warned me. She said the stories choose their translators. She said I should leave them in the box.
>
> I laughed. I am a philologist. I translate dead languages for breakfast. I do not believe in warnings from old women who smell of incense and obscurity.
>
> The first story is called **The Bell of Empty Hands**. It begins at a temple in Nara, in the year 1180, when a monk named Kūkai returned from Tang China with a bronze bell so large that it took fifty men to haul it up the mountain.
>
> But this is not a history. The story describes what the bell **heard** during the three days it lay at the bottom of the river, waiting to be recovered.
>
> I will translate it tomorrow. Tonight, I light a candle.
>
> —Y. Mori

Evelyn turned the page.

The original story began on the next leaf, written in a mixture of classical Chinese and Heian-era kana, with annotations in the margins in a different hand—the husband's, perhaps, or a later reader.

She read the first paragraph without translating:

> **當山階寺の僧、空海、唐よりの歸途、難波の津にて鐘を鑄す。鐘の聲、三千世界を震はす。されど鐘の內に、ひとりの女人の聲あり。**

Her Japanese was still halting, but she caught the gist. Kūkai. A bell. A woman's voice inside the bell.

She set the journal down.

The garden had gone dark. The koi had stopped moving. Somewhere in the distance, a train whistle—the Kyoto Line, running toward Osaka—cut across the night like a seam in time.

Evelyn lit a cigarette.

She had smoked since she was sixteen, a habit Robert had hated. He used to leave her ashtrays shaped like tambourines on the kitchen table, a silent campaign. She laughed now, thinking of it. The tambourines had been the first things she had thrown away after the funeral.

The first journal lay open in front of her.

She picked up her fountain pen—the one Robert had given her for Christmas in 1954, a Waterman 54 with a italic nib—and wrote the date in the margin.

**October 17, 1957.**

The same date. One year apart.

---

She translated the first story in three hours.

The Bell of Empty Hands was not about a bell. It was about a woman who had been buried alive beneath the temple foundation, a common offering in the old days when a structure was too grand to build with ordinary labor. Her name was O-Shizu. Her voice had been recorded by the bell's bronze walls in the form of a vibration so subtle that only another bell—or a very quiet person—could hear it.

Kūkai knew she was there. He had chosen the site. The stories said he had heard her sobbing during the casting and had inscribed a sutra on the bell's inner rim to give her peace.

But the sutra had not worked. O-Shizu was still there. Still sobbing. Still waiting for someone to dig her up.

Evelyn set her pen down.

The ink was smudged where her hand had trembled—not from fear, but from recognition. She had heard that sobbing before, in Cleveland, in Akron, in the silence between the phone ringing and the voice on the other end telling her Robert was gone.

She closed the journal.

---

At midnight, she heard footsteps on the gravel path.

She opened the shoji. Mori-san stood at the garden gate, holding a paper lantern that cast his face in amber.

"Did you finish?"

"Yes."

"And?"

"It is not a story." Evelyn looked at him. "It is a scream wrapped in language. Your wife translated it beautifully."

Mori-san did not smile. He reached into his sleeve and passed her a small lacquered box.

"For the second story. You must not open it until dawn."

"And if I do?"

"Then you will hear the bell."

He bowed. Turned. Walked back through the torii, his footsteps soft on the stone.

Evelyn stood in the doorway with the lacquered box in her hands.

The bell in the story had been cast in 1180. The woman beneath the temple had been silenced in 1180. The sutra had been inscribed in 1180.

And today was October 17, 1957.

Exactly one year since Y. Mori had begun her translation.

Evelyn closed the shoji. She sat on the tatami. She placed the lacquered box in front of her and did not open it.

Instead, she picked up the first journal and read the story again.

This time, she heard the sobbing.

Not as memory. As presence. As though O-Shizu were sitting behind her, breathless, waiting for the next word.

Evelyn turned the page.

The second story began:

> **The night the river ran backward.**

She read until dawn.
