# The Silence Between Words - Chapter 22: The Song of the Train

---

Twenty-second month.

Evelyn had been in Kyoto for three weeks when she heard the train.

Mori's house sat twenty minutes by foot from the nearest station, on a lane so quiet that the sound of someone coughing three houses away carried with the clarity of a sentence spoken in the same room. Trains did not pass near the house. The nearest track ran along the river, half a mile east, behind the old textile mill that had been converted into condominiums nobody wanted to buy.

And yet, on the twenty-second morning of her return, Evelyn heard it.

It came from nowhere and went nowhere, a low and rolling chord that vibrated in the floorboards and in the bones of her hands and in the spaces between the words Mori was telling her about a translation exercise he had set for his advanced students the previous autumn. She did not hear it at first. She thought it was the wind. Then she thought it was a truck. Then she thought it was nothing at all.

But Mori stopped speaking.

He turned his head toward the east. His left hand, which had been resting on the arm of his chair, lifted slightly — not a tremble, not a spasm, but a raise, as if saluting a flag only he could see.

" You hear it too," she said.

"I have been hearing it for years," he said.

"What is it?"

"A train. The midnight freight that runs along the old Kiso line. It passes the textile mill at 5:03. Always. On schedule."

"But it is 11:18."

"I know."

"How can you hear it then?"

Mori smiled. It was the smile of a man who had discovered something years ago and had been living inside the discovery ever since, who found it touching and slightly ridiculous that someone who had been awake for three weeks in his house had only just caught up to the obvious.

"Sometimes," he said, "the train is not on the track."

Evelyn did not laugh. Mori seldom joked about things that mattered, and when he did, his jokes were not invitations to laugh but instructions to look.

"I have been working with a student," he said, returning to the topic of the previous autumn's translation exercise, "who translates by listening. Not to the words. To what is behind the words. He says every sentence is a train, and the person who wrote it is standing on the platform, and the real meaning is not what the train says when it arrives but what it says when it passes — the horn, the whistle, the diminishing rumble, the silence it leaves behind."

"That is beautiful," Evelyn said.

"It is also wrong," Mori said. "And right. That is what makes it worth teaching."

He stood and went to the window. The garden was damp from a rain that had fallen while they were working and had stopped while they were not noticing. The plum tree was gone. The bush he had called kutsu ji was darker green than Evelyn remembered, almost black in the deep shadow of the eaves, as if the garden were absorbing light rather than reflecting it.

"The train is a translation," Mori said, still looking outward. "It brings something from one place to another. But it does not leave the same place it arrived at. Something changes in the transit. The freight may be the same. The destination may be the same. But the distance between them is not the same. The wear on the wheels is not the same. The person who sent the freight is not the same person who receives it, because time has passed and time changes everyone."

Evelyn stood and joined him at the window. The lane was empty, as it usually was at that hour, when the neighborhood had not yet fully left its sleep but was no longer entirely in it, that gray intermediate state where shadows have weight and colors have names.

"I have been thinking," she said, "about the list."

"I know."

"About the eighteenth entry."

"I did not finish it."

"I noticed."

Mori turned from the window. The light from the garden fell across his face, and Evelyn saw that he had been sitting in the low chair for two hours without moving, without taking his eyes from the paper in front of him, without asking her once whether she needed more tea. The left hand was still trembling, but less than it had been in the morning, as if the act of speaking about the unfinished sentence had calmed it, as if the sentence contained within it the seed of its own completion.

"It stopped," he said.

"What stopped?"

"The sentence. I was writing it, and I heard the train, and I stopped. Not because I had nothing left to say. Because I realized that what I had left to say was not something that could be said on paper. It was something that could only be said in the space between sentences. Between trains. Between two people sitting in a kitchen at 11:18 on a morning that had begun in Cleveland and was ending in Kyoto."

Evelyn reached out. She touched the sleeve of his robe. The fabric was rough cotton, worn thin at the cuffs by decades of hands that had used it to hold brushes and tea bowls and the edges of scrolls when the walls of the house had shifted in earthquakes and the roof had leaked in storms.

"Then do not finish it," she said.

"I must."

"No. You must not. A finished sentence is a finished silence. You have been living in the silence of that sentence for eighteen months. To finish it would be to end it."

Mori looked at her. For a moment, the kitchen was completely still. Even the clock in the corner, which had been ticking for seventy years, seemed to hold its breath. The train, which had already passed and was not coming again for another twenty-two hours, seemed to return and hover just outside the walls, as if curious to hear what a sentence ending in incomplete trust would sound like.

"You are a bold student," Mori said.

"I am a translator," she said. "I know when the sentence should pause."

"Come," he said, and this time the movement was not slow. He walked to the corner table where the kettle was kept. He lifted the lid. Steam rose, straight and steady. "The haiku is waiting."

They sat down again at the table. They placed the unfinished sentence — the list, the letter, the draft — to the side, where it belonged, where it would not be finished but would remain available for anyone who understood that some translations are not actions but states of being.

"The haiku," Mori said, and recited it from memory:

> *Old pond / frog jumps in / sound of water*

"The sound of water," Evelyn said. "That is the silence after the jump."

"No," Mori said. "That is the silence before it. The jump itself has no sound."

Evelyn sat with this for a long moment. The train was gone again, or had never been there, or was simply too far away to be distinguished from silence, and in that middle distance, between what had been said and what had not, she understood, briefly and completely, the thing she had come back to Kyoto to find.

Translation was not about moving words across a gap. It was about learning to sit at the edge of the gap and describe what was growing there.
