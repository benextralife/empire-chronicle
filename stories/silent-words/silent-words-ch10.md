# The Silence Between Words - Chapter 10: The City of Clocks

---

A month later, Evelyn was back in Cleveland.

The letter from Mori-san had been waiting at her old address—not the house in Akron, which she had sold, but the post office box she had kept open for emergencies. Inside was a single sheet of paper, Japanese characters on one side, English on the other:

> **You have finished the stories.**
> **The box is open.**
> **Now you must find the city of clocks.**
> **The next translator is waiting.**
>
> —S. Mori

Evelyn had bought a Greyhound ticket at the Osaka bus terminal, spent three nights on a train, and slept for twelve hours on the plane. Now she stood on Euclid Avenue, autumn air in her lungs, the smell of roasted chestnuts from a street vendor mixing with the exhaust from the hospital three blocks away.

Robert had worked at that hospital. He had been an orthopedic surgeon with hands that could set a femur in seventeen minutes flat. The nurses had called him *iron fingers*.

She had not visited since the funeral.

Today, she walked past the main entrance without going in.

---

The city of clocks was not in Cleveland.

She had thought it might be—a city of factories and gears, of the Cleveland Clock Company that had dominated the nineteenth century, of the huge municipal clock tower at Public Square that still struck the hours with a sound like heartbeats through stone.

But the journal had been specific: **a city where time is not measured but made.**

She found it in a thrift shop on West 6th.

The shop was called **Time Enough**, run by a man in his sixties who wore a bow tie and spoke in the cadence of someone who had learned to wait long ago. He was behind the counter, polishing a brass gear with a soft cloth.

"Looking for anything in particular?" he asked.

"A clock," Evelyn said. "But not a normal one. A clock that restores other clocks."

The man looked up. His eyes were blue, faded, the color of a sky that had seen too many indifferent winters. He set down his polishing cloth.

"We have those," he said. "Back room. Follow me."

---

The back room was larger than the shop itself, lined floor to ceiling with clocks of every description—grandfather clocks, cuckoo clocks, carriage clocks, wristwatches still ticking in their velvet cases, a single massive regulator clock from the 1840s that dominated the far wall like a bishop on a chessboard.

"These are my patients," the man said. He ran a hand along the side of a bracket clock whose mainspring had clearly not seen oil since the Coolidge administration. "I fix them. I restore them. Sometimes I talk to them. They talk back."

"And the one I'm looking for?"

He stopped at a workbench in the corner. On it lay a clock unlike any Evelyn had ever seen—small, made of dark wood inlaid with mother-of-pearl, its face a single number: **XII**. No hands. No gears visible. No winding key.

"This is the one," he said. "It stopped three hundred years ago. It has not moved since."

"What time does it say?"

He smiled. "It does not say time. It says **translation**. The face changes depending on who looks at it. To a horologist, it looks like a diagram of an escapement mechanism. To a poet, it looks like a stanza. To a restorer—"

"—it looks like an instruction manual," Evelyn finished.

He nodded. "You are the restorer."

"How did you know?"

"I didn't. The clock did." He gestured to the bench. "It has been waiting. It told me this morning that its mechanic was coming. It was right. You are the mechanic."

Evelyn sat on the stool. The clock was heavy in her hands, the wood smooth, the mother-of-pearl cool against her palms. She turned it over.

On the back, carved so finely she could barely feel it with her fingertip, was a single word in a script she recognized from the marginalia of the first journal:

**譯**

Translate.

The clock was not broken.
It was **full**.
It had been holding a story for three centuries, wound too tight to run, waiting for someone with the patience to unwind it.

She opened her tool kit. She had carried it since her grandfather's death—a set of miniature screwdrivers, a magnifying visor, a jar of clock oil, a brass brush. She had used it to fix ten-dollar watches from the backs of pharmacies and twenty-dollar clocks from estate sales.

She had never used it on anything like this.

The old man watched without interfering.

Evelyn began to work.

---

She worked for three days.

She did not leave the shop. The owner brought her food—sandwiches from the deli next door, apples, coffee that tasted like burnt sugar. She ate standing up, her eyes on the workbench, her hands moving with a precision she had not known she possessed.

On the second day, she found the secret compartment.

It was behind the clock's mainplate, disguised as a decorative filigree, opened by pressing a hidden jewel at the base of the XII. Inside lay a single strip of paper, no wider than her thumb, with writing so small she needed her magnifying visor to read it.

The handwriting was her grandfather's.

> **To the one who finds this:**
>
> **The clock does not measure time. It measures attention. Every time you fix a broken thing, you add a second. Every time you listen to something that cannot speak, you add a minute. Every time you translate a story that is not yours, you add an hour.**
>
> **I have added thirty years.**
>
> **My wife added eleven.**
>
> **My mother added none. She did not listen.**
>
> **Do not make her mistake.**
>
> **The clock is you.**
> **The story is the gear.**
> **The silence between words is the escapement.**
>
> **Wind it when you are ready.**
> **But know this: once it runs, it will not stop until the last story is told. And you will be the one telling it.**

Evelyn set down the strip of paper.

Her hands were steady. Her breathing was even. The old man was asleep in the corner, his head on a pile of clock parts.

She reassembled the clock. She oiled the mainspring. She aligned the escapement with a touch so light it was barely a touch at all.

She pressed the jewel at the base of XII.

The clock began to tick.

---

The sound was ordinary. Mundane. The tick of any mechanism measuring the seconds between one breath and the next.

But Evelyn heard more.

She heard the monk's bell, tolling in a temple that no longer existed.
She heard the river running forward, carrying a woman's voice that had been silenced for three hundred years.
She heard her grandfather's breath, caught between Go moves in a tower that folded into itself like a dream.
She heard Robert's laugh, recorded in the tambourines she had thrown away, recovered now, restored, reviving.

The clock on the workbench was not keeping time.

It was keeping **witness**.

Evelyn sat back on her stool.

The old man opened one eye. "It runs," he said. Not a question. A statement.

"Yes."

"And?"

"It's not a clock," she said. "It's a chorus."

He nodded, closed his eye, and went back to sleep.

Evelyn sat in the quiet of the back room, listening to the tick that held every story she had ever lived and every story she had yet to tell. The next translator was coming. The city of clocks was waking up.

She picked up her pen.

She began to write.
