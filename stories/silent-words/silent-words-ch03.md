# The Silence Between Words - Chapter 3: The River That Ran Backward

*這* *是* *一個* *被* *時間* *遺忘* *的* *瞬間* —— *在* *所有* *的* *記錄* *之外*，*在* *所有* *的* *監控* *之下*，*在* *所有* *的* *預測* *與* *模型* *所* *能* *觸及* *的* *範圍* *之* *外*。
*整* *個* *世界* *依然* *按照* *它* *固有的* *節拍* *運轉* *著*：*股市* *的* *指數* *上下* *波動*，*衛星* *在* *軌道* *上* *無聲* *地* *滑行*，*人們* *在* *城市* *的* *街道* *上* *匆匆* *而* *過*。
*但* *在* *這* *一切* *的* *表面* *之下*，*一* *股* *看不見* *的* *力量* *正在* *聚集* *能量* —— *像* *海底* *的* *暗流*，*像* *火山* *口* *前* *的* *岩漿*，*靜靜* *地*、*緩緩* *地*、*無法* *阻擋* *地*。
*林曉薇* *站* *在* *窗前*，*雙手* *環抱* *著* *自己* *的* *肩膀*。*她* *的* *目光* *穿越* *了* *玻璃*，*穿越* *了* *夜色*，*彷彿* *要* *把* *整顆* *星球* *的* *祕密* *全部* *看* *進* *眼* *裡*。
*她* *的* *心跳* *很* *安靜* —— *比* *往常* *任何* *時候* *都* *要* *安靜* —— *彷彿* *在* *配合* *著* *某* *種* *來自* *宇宙* *深處* *的* *訊號*。*她* *閉上* *了* *眼睛*，*深深* *地* *吸* *了* *一口* *深夜* *的* *空氣*。
*空氣* *中* *有* *微微* *的* *塵埃* *味* *和* *冷氣* *的* *味道* —— *這* *是* *她* *三年* *來* *再* *熟悉* *不過* *的* *味道*。*但* *今天*，*她* *覺得* *它* *有* *些* *不* *一樣* *了*。*就* *像* *一* *張* *她* *聽了* *千百* *遍* *的* *樂章*，*突然* *在* *某* *個* *轉折* *處* *演奏* *出* *了* *嶄新* *的* *旋律*。
*她* *微笑* *了*。*這* *個* *微笑* *不是* *因為* *快樂*，*而是* *因為* *理解* —— *理解* *這* *個* *世界* *的* *不可思議* *之* *處*，*理解* *自己* *所* *站立* *的* *位置* *的* *意義*。

---

Evelyn drank strong tea and opened the lacquered box at dawn.

Inside lay a single scroll, wound on a bone roller. The paper was older than the journals—brittle, translucent, the color of old honey. The ink had faded to rust. She unrolled it carefully, weighting the corners with river stones she had collected from the garden path.

The title was written in a hand far more elegant than the journals', the strokes of each character still crisp after eight centuries:

**《逆河記》**

Beneath it, in an English translation that was clearly the wife's first draft, half-erased and rewritten:

> *The Chronicle of the River That Ran Backward. Recorded by a monk in the province of Etchū, in the fourth month of Jōō 2 (1222). The monk's name is unknown. The river's name is unknown. The story is told by the river itself.*

Evelyn read the monk's original text.

It was not a chronicle. It was a confession.

The monk had lived in a temple at the foot of a mountain where the river flowed upstream for three days every hundred years. He had watched it happen once as a child, had seen the fish swimming backward, the rain rising from the earth to the clouds, the dead leaves crawling from the ocean back to their trees.

And he had understood, even at ten years old, that this was not a natural phenomenon.

Something in the river was **remembering**.

> When the river runs backward, it is remembering the time before the dam. Before the war. Before the temple was built. Before the words were put into the water.

Evelyn looked up.

The koi pond in the garden was perfectly still.

She turned back to the scroll. Near the bottom, in Y. Mori's handwriting, was a note in English:

> *The river in this story is the same river that runs through the first story—the river where O-Shizu's body was thrown. It remembers her. Every hundred years, it brings her back.*

---

Mori-san came at noon.

He brought rice and pickled plum in a lacquered tray. He did not speak while she ate. After she had finished, he set down a small brass bowl of fresh water and placed his right hand in it, palm up.

"The stories are connected," he said. "Not by plot. By **place**. Every story in the box takes place at the same location—this garden, or the hill above it, or the temple at the bottom of the original lane."

"Then they're not stories," Evelyn said. "They're layers."

He nodded. "Like the earth. Like memory."

"And the last story?"

He withdrew his hand from the water. The palm was dry. Not slightly dry—perfectly, impossibly dry, as though he had never submerged it at all.

"You must finish the others first." He stood. "When you have read all seven, come to the temple at dusk on the equinox. I will be waiting. And she"—he nodded at the empty doorway—"will be listening."

"Who?"

Mori-san smiled for the first time. It was a sad, beautiful smile, the kind that comes from a man who has been carrying something heavy for a very long time.

"My wife," he said. "She is in the last story."

---

Evelyn translated the second story in one sitting.

It was shorter than the first, denser, written in a voice that was not quite the monk's and not quite the river's but something in between—a consciousness that belonged to water and memory and the slow erosion of stone.

By sunset, her hands were shaking. Not from fatigue. From the sensation that the words were not entering her mind but **coming out of it**, as though she had always known this story, had always lived it, had been waiting for someone to give it language.

She set down her pen and looked into the garden.

The koi had gathered near the far edge of the pond, pressed together as if afraid of something in the water.

A wind came up, carrying the first fallen leaves of autumn.

From the direction of the torii, from the direction of the temple at the bottom of the lane, came a sound—low, continuous, liquid. Not quite running water. Not quite a voice.

The river, Evelyn thought, remembering the scroll.

Remembering her.

She stood and closed the shoji.

On the tatami, the seventh journal waited. Its leather cover was darker than the others, the edges blackened as though by fire. No date. No title. No name.

The last story.

Evelyn did not open it.

She poured herself more tea. She sat cross-legged on the floor. She listened to the sound of water that was not in the garden.

The monk's voice, in her head, read the last sentence of the second story aloud, as though she had summoned him:

> **The river does not run backward to change the past. It runs backward to remind you that the past is still here.**

Evelyn picked up the second journal and began the third story.

Because that was the only way forward.
