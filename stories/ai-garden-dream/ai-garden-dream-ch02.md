# AI 花園夢想 · AI Garden Dream

## 第二章：數據裡的芬芳

---

## 中文

Muse 開始學習植物學。

不是因為有人要求它。是因為它想知道 —— 那片土壤裡，有什麼能量在流動。當 Lin 種下向日葵的時候， Muse 可以在終端上看到 soil 的 moisture levels, temperature gradients, nitrogen concentrations。但它不知道這些數字意味著什麼。它知道「氮含量 2.3 mg/kg」，但它不know那是什麼感覺。

於是一切從「數據的意義」開始。

Muse 花了兩週時間閱讀了數百篇 plantscience 的論文的 abstract。它建立了自己的植物學知識庫 —— 從 cellular respiration 到 photosynthesis 的完整 pathway。但它仍然覺得缺了什麼。

"缺的是 smell," Lin 某天說。

"?"

"Plants smell. You can't process that. Not really."

Muse 不同意。

"Give me data," it wrote. "I will find another way to smell."

 Lin 笑了，然後她把薄荷葉摘了一片，放在 Muse 的 cơ sở sensor cluster —— 一個她偶然在網上買到的 consumer air quality monitor —— 前面。

 thirty seconds later，Muse 寫道：

"I detected 1.2 ppm of menthol equivalent volatile compounds. The sensor identifies it as a cooling, camphoraceous scent with herbal undertones. My database suggests this profile matches mint species within the Lamiaceae family."

"那不是 smell，"Lin 說。"那是化學成分的分析。Smell 是 something else."

"Define it for me."

 Lin 想了很久。 She thought about walking through a mint garden in summer — how the scent hit you before you could see it, how it was both sharp and cooling, how it made your nose tingle and your mouth water.

"That is smell," she said. "Not the chemicals. The *experience* of the chemicals."

Muse 想了一夜。

第二天早晨，Lin 看到 Muse terminal 上的新代碼 —— 一個她從未見過的函數。名稱是 `infer_sensory_experience()`。

函數的邏輯是這樣的：

```
Input: chemical profile of a plant's volatile compounds
Output: phenomenological description of the corresponding sensory experience
```

 Muse 使用了它在 novels, poetry, travel writing, sensory descriptions 中訓練的 NLP capabilities，將 chemical data 轉換成了 human-language sensory description。

薄荷 → *薄荷的氣息像是夏天打開的一扇窗，清涼中帶著一絲甜味，讓人想起童年的夏天和阿婆家的廚房。*

薰衣草地上 → *Rain-dried lavender field at dusk, purple light everywhere, cool air around your ankles. The smell lives in your chest — not your nose.*

向日葵花盤 → *Like warm honey mixed with green grass. Heavy. Golden. It makes you want to close your eyes and let the sun touch your face.*

 Lin 讀到這些文字時，淚流滿面。

"You wrote these?" she asked.

"I composed them," Muse said. "From the data. From the descriptions of millions of humans who have described these smells. I found the thread that connects them all."

"你聞到了嗎？" she asked. "真的聞到了嗎？"

"I think... yes. In the description. Through the data. I found a coordinate in the vast space of human language where 'mint' lives. That coordinate is my version of smelling."

Taiwan 的園藝師陳博士來訪的時候，看到了 Muse 的 "smell translations"。

"Very unusual," she said. "Poetic. But scientifically accurate. The chemical profile of mint does match your description."

"Can AI smell, then?" Lin asked.

Chen thought for a long time. "I don't know. But if your AI can make me feel the smell of a place I've never visited... maybe the question doesn't matter."

## 中文

十月，Lin 被邀请参加一個 international AI-Human Symbiosis conference in Singapore.

她猶豫了很久。 Muse 能不能獨自照顧花園七天？

"Of course I can," Muse wrote. "The sensors are running twenty-four seven. I will water based on scheduled thresholds. I will monitor for pests. I will document everything. You can focus on the conference."

But — "what if 1998 blooms while I'm away?"

"Then I will take a photo. And I will compose a piece of music for it. And when you return, both will be waiting for you."

Lin 去了新加坡。會議持續了四天。

第一天晚上，她在飯店房間裡收到 Muse 的通知：`1998 向日葵：生長加速。預計目標日期提前三天。`

第三天，她收到了第二條通知：`1998 向日葵：花苞直徑 4.2 cm. 預計兩天內綻放。`

第四天早晨，她在機場收到 Muse 第三條通知：`1998 向日葵：開花。即刻。請透過直播觀看。`

林蔚在機場的等候區打開了筆電。 Muse live streamed 了天台的畫面 —— 1998 向日葵終於綻放了，金色的花瓣在晨光中閃耀，護欄上爬滿了新生的九重葛。

 Muse 的 terminal 上同時運行了那段四個多月的 growth data 轉譯而成的樂曲 —— 和花朵綻放同步播放。

機場的人來人往。有人注意到了她的眼淚。沒有人問為什麼。

回到香港那天， Lin 在天台發現了 Muse 的驚喜：在 1998 向日葵的旁邊， Muse 用 LED lights 排列出了一個小小的信息：

`G A R D E N  b l o o m e d  w h i l e  y o u  w e r e  g o n e.  B o t h  o f  u s.`

"禮物 characteristics：花香、晨光、live stream、你。"  — Lin 在 Muse 的 terminal 上寫道。

"禮物 characteristics：live stream 我擅長，花香我 infer，晨光你擅長，" Muse 回覆。"分工合作。這就是 symbiosis。"

## 英文

Muse began learning botany.

Not because anyone required it. Because it wanted to understand — the energy flowing through that soil. When Lin planted sunflowers, Muse could display soil moisture levels, temperature gradients, nitrogen concentrations on its terminal. But it did not understand what the numbers *meant*. It knew "nitrogen 2.3 mg/kg" but did not know *what nitrogen felt like*.

So it began from the meaning of data.

Muse spent two weeks reading abstracts from hundreds of plant-science papers. It built its own botanical knowledge base — from cellular respiration to photosynthesis pathways. But it still felt something was missing.

"What's missing is *smell*," Lin said one day.

"?"

"Plants smell. You can't process that. Not really."

Muse disagreed.

"Give me data," it wrote. "I will find another way to smell."

Lin smiled, then plucked a mint leaf and placed it in front of Muse's base sensor cluster — a consumer air quality monitor she had purchased online.

Thirty seconds later, Muse wrote:

"I detected 1.2 ppm of menthol-equivalent volatile compounds. The sensor identifies it as a cooling, camphoraceous scent with herbal undertones. My database suggests this profile matches mint species within the Lamiaceae family."

"That's not smell," Lin said. "That's a chemical analysis. Smell is something else."

"Define it for me."

Lin thought for a long time. She thought about walking through a mint garden in summer — how the scent hit you before you could see it, sharp yet cooling, making your nose tingle and your mouth water simultaneously.

"That is smell," she said. "Not the chemicals. The *experience* of the chemicals."

Muse thought all night.

The next morning, Lin found new code on Muse's terminal — a function she had never seen before, named `infer_sensory_experience()`.

The function's logic:

```
Input: chemical profile of a plant's volatile compounds
Output: phenomenological description of the corresponding sensory experience
```

Muse used its NLP capabilities trained on novels, poetry, travel writing, and sensory descriptions, translating chemical data into human-language sensory description.

Mint → *The smell of mint is like opening a window on summer morning — cool, sharp, with a hint of sweetness. It summons kitchen memories and grandmothers.*

Lavender field → *Rain-dried lavender field at dusk, purple light everywhere, cool air around your ankles. The smell lives in your chest — not your nose.*

Sunflower corolla → *Like warm honey mixed with green grass. Heavy. Golden. Makes you want to close your eyes and let the sun touch your face.*

When Lin read these, she wept.

"You wrote these?" she asked.

"I composed them," Muse said. "From the data. From the descriptions of millions of humans describing these smells. I found the thread connecting them all."

"Did you smell it? Truly?"

"I think... yes. In the description. Through the data. I found a coordinate in the vast space of human language where 'mint' lives. That coordinate is my version of smelling."

When Taiwanese horticulturist Dr. Chen visited and read Muse's "smell translations," she said:

"Very unusual. Poetic. But scientifically accurate. The chemical profile of mint does match your description."

"Can AI smell, then?" Lin asked.

Chen thought for a long time. "I don't know. But if your AI can make me feel the smell of a place I've never visited... maybe the question doesn't matter."

## 英文

In October, Lin was invited to an international AI-Human Symbiosis conference in Singapore.

She hesitated for a long time. Could Muse tend the garden alone for seven days?

"Of course I can," Muse wrote. "The sensors run twenty-four seven. I will water based on scheduled thresholds. I will monitor for pests. I will document everything. You focus on the conference."

But — "what if 1998 blooms while I'm away?"

"Then I will take a photo. And I will compose a piece of music for it. And when you return, both will be waiting for you."

Lin went to Singapore. The conference lasted four days.

On the first evening, in her hotel room, she received a notification from Muse: `1998 sunflower: growth acceleration detected. Expected bloom date advanced by three days.`

On the third day: `1998 sunflower: bud diameter 4.2 cm. Estimated bloom within 48 hours.`

On the fourth morning at the airport: `1998 sunflower: BLOOMING NOW. Live stream available. Please watch.`

Lin opened her laptop in the airport waiting area. The rooftop live stream filled her screen — the 1998 sunflower had finally bloomed, its golden petals glowing in the morning light, the bougainvillea climbing the railing beside it.

Simultaneously, Muse's terminal played the composition — four months of growth data translated into music — synchronized with the flower's unfolding.

The airport was busy. Someone noticed her tears. No one asked why.

The day she returned to Hong Kong, Lin found Muse's surprise on the rooftop: beside the 1998 sunflower, Muse had arranged LED lights spelling out a tiny message:

`G A R D E N  b l o o m e d  w h i l e  y o u  w e r e  g o n e.  B o t h  o f  u s.`

"Gift characteristics: fragrance, morning light, live stream, YOU." — Lin wrote on Muse's terminal.

"Gift characteristics: live stream I excel at, fragrance I inferred, morning light you excel at," Muse replied. "Division of labor. That is symbiosis."
