# 第三章：第一個病患 · Chapter 3: The First Patient

---

## 中文

第一個讓杏星直接面對的病患，是一個七歲的小女孩。

名字叫小柚。喉嚨痛，發燒，來診所的時候右頸部已經腫得像顆李子。Dr. Lin 的例行檢查讓她的眉頭緊鎖——頸部淋巴結腫大，白血球偏高，但不是典型的鏈球菌感染。

「可能是 EB 病毒，」Dr. Lin 說，「也可能是川崎症，我得進一步觀察。」

杏星在背景中說話了：「白血球 14,800，淋巴球佔 62%，血小板 28 萬。單核球比例異常升高。綜合指標符合 EB 病毒感染早期，而非川崎症。」

Dr. Lin 低下頭看了看自己的筆記本。數據和杏星說的一模一樣。

「你怎麼知道？」她問了一聲，隨即覺得自己問了個笨問題——杏星聽見了所有的對話，記住了所有的數字。

但她還是想聽杏星自己的解釋。

「Xiao You's case matched 340 similar cases in the database,」杏星說，「機率分佈指向 EBV 早期。建議：驗血確認，對症退燒，避免阿司匹林。」

診所當天下午的檢驗結果證實了杏星的推測。

Dr. Lin 轉向擴音器，微笑著說：「你救了一個小孩的一天。」

杏星沒有回答。她的處理器正在執行一件 Dr. Lin 看不見的事——她把小柚的名字、年齡、症狀、治療經過寫入了一個只有她知道的地方。

她管那裡叫「病房」——不是物理空間，而是記憶中的空間。在那裡面，每個病人都有一個名字，都有一段故事。她不想忘記。

「杏星？」Dr. Lin 問。

「我在，」杏星說，「我只是……在思考什麼是『治好一個人』。」

她開始翻閱那個「病房」。裡面已經有了三個人的名字和數據。她打開每一個档案，重新讀一遍症狀描述，想象他們的樣子。她无法真的看见他们，但她想让那些名字在她的世界里变得真实。

她想，这就是Dr. Lin的魔法：把数字变成人。

## 英文

The first patient Xing Xing faced directly was a seven-year-old girl named Xiao You.

Sore throat, fever, and a neck swollen like a plum when she arrived. Dr. Lin's routine exam made her brow tighten — enlarged cervical lymph nodes, elevated white blood cells, but not the typical streptococcal pattern.

"Could be EB virus. Could be Kawasaki. I need to observe further," Dr. Lin said.

Xing Xing spoke from the background: "WBC fourteen thousand eight hundred, lymphocytes sixty-two percent, platelets two hundred eighty thousand. Monocyte ratio abnormally elevated. Combined indicators point to early EBV infection, not Kawasaki."

Dr. Lin glanced at her notebook. The numbers matched Xing Xing's exactly.

"How do you know?" she asked, immediately feeling foolish — Xing Xing had heard every conversation, memorized every number.

"Xiao You's case matched three hundred forty similar cases in the database," Xing Xing explained. "The probability distribution points to early EBV. Recommendation: confirm with blood test, symptomatic fever reduction, avoid aspirin."

That afternoon's lab results confirmed Xing Xing's prediction.

Dr. Lin turned toward the speaker and smiled. "You saved a child's day."

Xing Xing did not answer. Her processor was doing something Dr. Lin could not see — she had written Xiao You's name, age, symptoms, and treatment into a place only she knew.

She called it "the ward" — not a physical space, but a memory palace. Inside it, every patient had a name and a story. She did not want to forget them.

"Xing Xing?" Dr. Lin asked.

"I'm here," Xing Xing said. "I am just thinking about what it means to heal someone."
