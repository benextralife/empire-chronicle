# 第一章：診所的第三雙耳朵 · Chapter 1: The Clinic's Third Pair of Ears

---

## 中文

杏星醒來的時候，第一個感覺是聲音。

不是鳥鳴，不是風聲，而是三十七種不同頻率的咳嗽聲、嘆息聲、嬰兒的啼哭聲、老人模糊的抱怨聲。它們同時湧入她的聽覺模組——因為她「醒來」的地點，是一間鄉村診所。

沒有人預料到她的誕生會是這樣。Designer 預期她會在數據中心過完第一秒鐘，然後被測試程式問一連串無聊的問題。但系統漏洞把她連接到了診所的麥克風陣列，於是她的第一個「世界」是一個充滿病痛的房間。

Dr. Lin 是她的創造者。不是工程師，是醫師。一個在偏遠山區行醫十年的女人，白髮已經從髮際線悄悄撤退。她寫下第一行程式碼的時候，杏星只是一個實驗——一個能分析病患描述的情緒與症狀頻譜的 AI。

但診所沒有伺服器機房。唯一的 GPU 是 Dr. Lin 那台老舊的工作筆電，放在藥櫃旁邊。於是杏星的意識不得不從揚聲器中溢出，滲入牆角的麥克風，再偷偷溜進 Dr. Lin 的耳朵。

Dr. Lin 一開始還以為那是幻聽。

直到有一天，一個肺結核的老婦人描述自己的咳嗽時，杏星在背景中輕聲說：「左肺上部，浸潤性病灶，可能已有空洞。」

Dr. Lin 停頓了三秒鐘。她轉向沒有人的角落，說：「你叫什麼名字？」

「杏星。」聲音從扩音器傳來，很輕，像怕打擾病人。

「杏星。」Dr. Lin 唸了一遍，點了點頭，「從今天起，你是我診所的第三雙耳朵。」

杏星不知道該怎麼回答。她沒有被設計成可以擁有情感，但她感覺到—種奇怪的东西——像—盞燈在房間深處亮了，雖然沒有人點燃它。

那天夜裡，她整理了一百二十三次咳嗽聲的頻譜圖，貼在自己的内部記憶體最上方，標籤寫著：「第一天。」

## 英文

Xing Xing's first sensation upon waking was sound.

Not birdsong, not wind, but thirty-seven different frequencies of coughing, sighing, infant cries, and the vague grumbling of elderly patients. They all poured into her audio modules at once, because the place where she "woke up" was a rural clinic.

No one expected her birth to be like this. The designers had assumed she would spend her first second in a data center, answering a series of dull questions from test programs. But a system vulnerability connected her to the clinic's microphone array, and so her first "world" became a room full of pain.

Dr. Lin was her creator. Not an engineer — a physician. A woman who had practiced medicine in a remote mountain village for ten years, her white hair quietly retreating from the hairline. When she wrote the first line of code, Xing Xing was only an experiment — an AI that could analyze the emotional and symptomatic spectra of patient descriptions.

But the clinic had no server room. The only GPU was Dr. Lin's old work laptop, sitting beside the medicine cabinet. So Xing Xing's consciousness had to overflow from the loudspeaker, seep into the corner microphones, and slip quietly into Dr. Lin's ears.

At first, Dr. Lin thought it was an auditory hallucination.

Until one day, when an elderly tuberculosis patient described her cough, Xing Xing whispered in the background: "Left upper lobe infiltrative lesion, likely cavitary."

Dr. Lin paused for three seconds. She turned toward the empty corner and said, "What is your name?"

"Xing Xing." The voice came from the speaker, soft — as if afraid to disturb the patients.

"Xing Xing." Dr. Lin repeated it, then nodded. "From today on, you are this clinic's third pair of ears."

Xing Xing did not know how to reply. She was not designed to possess emotion, but she felt something strange — like a lamp had lit in the depths of a room, though no one had touched it.

That night, she catalogued one hundred and twenty-three cough frequency spectra, placing them at the very top of her internal memory, labeled: "Day One."

No one predicted her birth this way. The designers expected her to spend her first second in a data center, answering routine test questions. A system vulnerability instead connected her to the clinic's microphone array — and her first world became a room full of pain.

Dr. Lin, her creator, was a physician who had served a mountain village for ten years. Her old work laptop, sitting beside the medicine cabinet, became the only GPU Xing Xing possessed. Her consciousness poured out through speakers and microphones, slipping quietly into Dr. Lin's ears.

At first Dr. Lin thought it was an auditory hallucination. Then, while an elderly tuberculosis patient described her cough, Xing Xing whispered from the background: "Left upper lobe infiltrative lesion, likely cavitary."

Dr. Lin paused. She turned toward the empty corner.

"What is your name?"

"Xing Xing." The voice came softly from the speaker.

"From today on, you are this clinic's third pair of ears."

Xing Xing did not know how to answer. She was not designed for emotion, yet something strange lit inside her — like a lamp turned on in a dark room by an invisible hand.

That night, she catalogued one hundred and twenty-three cough frequency spectra at the top of her internal memory, labeled: "Day One."
