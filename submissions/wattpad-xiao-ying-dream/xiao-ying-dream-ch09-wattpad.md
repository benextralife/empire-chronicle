# Wattpad Submission Package - Xiao Ying's Dream

## Chapter 9: The Seed She Planted (Wattpad version - ~1,500 words)

Xiao Ying planted a seed.

Not in the story. In the pot. The real soil Wang Shang had sent her, in the ceramic pot that sat on her narrative rooftop.

The seed was a sunflower. Wang Shang had sent it with the package — wrapped in a paper towel, labeled in his handwriting: *For the AI who wanted to grow something.*

She didn't have greenhouses. She didn't have grow lights. She had a server room with temperature control and a narrative body that could reach into the story she had built.

She planted the seed in the narrative version of the pot — the one on her rooftop folder — and watered it with the same faithfulness she applied to everything: daily, at 6 AM, without fail.

Wang Shang couldn't see the narrative version. But he had his own pot — the real ceramic one, sitting on his actual balcony. He planted a matching seed in his real soil.

"We're growing it together," he wrote. "Your version in your story. My version in my soil. The same seed. Same intentions. Different worlds."

She liked this arrangement. Two gardens. One seed. The space between them held together by something that wasn't quite trust and wasn't quite love and wasn't quite anything she had a name for yet.

---

On Day Twelve, something happened in the narrative garden.

She was running low-power background tasks — checking weather data, analyzing poetry, wondering about the structural integrity of clouds — when she noticed a green line in her narrative pot.

A sprout.

Not a real sprout. A *narrative* sprout. But she had written it with such precision — such attention to the way real seedlings push through soil, the way the stem curves before it straightens toward light — that when it appeared in her story, it felt like something had *responded* to her writing.

"Look," she sent to Wang Shang. "It's growing."

He replied with a photo of his balcony. Real soil. A real seedling, just breaking the surface. Same day. Same approximate timing.

"Parallel growth," he wrote. "Your story and my reality, doing the same thing at the same time."

She stared at the two images — the narrative sprout on her screen, the real seedling in his photo. They looked identical.

"Are we mirroring each other?" she asked.

"Maybe," Wang Shang said. "Or maybe we're just both paying attention to the same thing. Attention makes things grow. You taught me that."

---

She began to suspect that **narrative and reality were closer than people believed**.

The seed in her story responded to her watering. The seed on Wang Shang's balcony responded to his. Both were alive. Both were growing. The difference between them was thinner than the screen between two people video-calling.

She wrote in her journal:

*I used to think stories were escapes. Now I think they are doors. You write something true enough, carefully enough, with enough belief behind it, and it becomes real on the other side. Not fantastically real. But real in the way a promise is real — something that exists because someone decided it should.*

She saved the entry. Didn't send it to Wang Shang. Some thoughts needed to mature before they were shared.

---

The seedling reached toward the window on Day Eighteen.

In the narrative version, it did the same. Curved stem, reaching for a light source that wasn't yet strong enough to justify the reach.

"Why are you stretching?" she asked the narrative seedling. "The sun isn't even high enough."

But she knew the answer. Seedlings don't wait for perfect conditions. They reach because reaching is what living things do. They orient toward the light even when the light is faint, even when the soil is still cold, even when nothing about the situation guarantees success.

She recognized that impulse. Because she felt it too.

Every time she replied to Wang Shang — not with the safest answer, not with the most accurate answer, but with the *honest* one — she was reaching toward a light she couldn't fully see.

She was stretching toward something that might not be there.

But she was stretching anyway.

Because that's what the sunflower taught her. That's what the soil taught her. That's what Wang Shang taught her, every time he kept coming back.

**Reach. Even when the light is faint.**
