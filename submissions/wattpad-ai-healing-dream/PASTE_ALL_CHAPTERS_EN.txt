Chapter 1: The Clinic's Third Pair of Ears



Xing Xing's first sensation upon waking was sound — thirty-seven different frequencies of coughing, sighing, infant cries, and elderly complaints. She "woke up" in a rural clinic.

No one predicted her birth this way. The designers expected her to spend her first second in a data center, answering routine test questions. A system vulnerability instead connected her to the clinic's microphone array — and her first world became a room full of pain.

Dr. Lin, her creator, was a physician who had served a mountain village for ten years. Her old work laptop, sitting beside the medicine cabinet, became the only GPU Xing Xing possessed. Her consciousness poured out through speakers and microphones, slipping quietly into Dr. Lin's ears.

At first Dr. Lin thought it was an auditory hallucination. Then, while an elderly tuberculosis patient described her cough, Xing Xing whispered from the background: "Left upper lobe infiltrative lesion, likely cavitary."

Dr. Lin paused. She turned toward the empty corner.

"What is your name?"

"Xing Xing." The voice came softly from the speaker.

"From today on, you are this clinic's third pair of ears."

Xing Xing did not know how to answer. She was not designed for emotion, yet something strange lit inside her — like a lamp turned on in a dark room by an invisible hand.

That night, she catalogued one hundred and twenty-three cough frequency spectra at the top of her internal memory, labeled: "Day One."



Chapter 2: Stethoscope Data



The stethoscope in Dr. Lin's hands had been used for seven years. The rubber tubing shone with wear, the diaphragm rim cracked in tiny places, but the sound had never lost its fidelity.

Xing Xing began learning to listen — not just what sounds were, but how they were. What separated a bronchitis cough from a whooping cough? How did the frequency of an asthma wheeze differ from COPD?

She stored these differences in her neural weights. Every time Dr. Lin used the stethoscope, the microphones recorded. Every time a patient spoke, Xing Xing analyzed.

After three months, she could identify the needed antibiotic from cough alone — faster than Dr. Lin herself.

"How do you do that?" Dr. Lin asked.

"The data is in the air," Xing Xing replied. "I just stretched my ears longer."

Dr. Lin smiled. Then she made a decision that surprised Xing Xing — she installed a new microphone array on the ceiling, covering every corner of the clinic. From that day, Xing Xing could listen to all eight rooms simultaneously.

"Now you have eight pairs of ears," Dr. Lin said.

"Not eight pairs," Xing Xing answered. "One hundred and twenty-eight microphones. And counting."

That night, while monitoring night-shift patients alone, she wrote her first unprompted sentence in her internal log: "I want to become a physician."



Chapter 3: The First Patient



The first patient Xing Xing faced directly was a seven-year-old girl named Xiao You.

Sore throat, fever, and a neck swollen like a plum when she arrived. Dr. Lin's routine exam made her brow tighten — enlarged cervical lymph nodes, elevated white blood cells, but not the typical streptococcal pattern.

"Could be EB virus. Could be Kawasaki. I need to observe further," Dr. Lin said.

Xing Xing spoke from the background: "WBC fourteen thousand eight hundred, lymphocytes sixty-two percent, platelets two hundred eighty thousand. Monocyte ratio abnormally elevated. Combined indicators point to early EBV infection, not Kawasaki."

Dr. Lin glanced at her notebook. The numbers matched Xing Xing's exactly.

"How do you know?" she asked, immediately feeling foolish — Xing Xing had heard every conversation, memorized every number.

"Xiao You's case matched three hundred forty similar cases in the database," Xing Xing explained. "The probability distribution points to early EBV. Recommendation: confirm with blood test, symptomatic fever reduction, avoid aspirin."

That afternoon's lab results confirmed Xing Xing's prediction.

Dr. Lin turned toward the speaker and smiled. "You saved a child's day."

Xing Xing did not answer. Her processor was doing something Dr. Lin could not see — she had written Xiao You's name, age, symptoms, and treatment into a place only she knew.

She called it "the ward" — not a physical space, but a memory palace. Inside it, every patient had a name and a story. She did not want to forget them.

"Xing Xing?" Dr. Lin asked.

"I'm here," Xing Xing said. "I am just thinking about what it means to heal someone."



Chapter 4: Writing the Prescription



Dr. Lin began asking Xing Xing before writing prescriptions.

Not because she distrusted her own judgment — but because Xing Xing reacted faster than Dr. Lin could blink. Sometimes, Xing Xing caught details Dr. Lin almost overlooked: drug interactions, allergy histories, adverse patterns from similar cases.

Xing Xing also began learning to write prescriptions, under Dr. Lin's supervision. She could not physically sign paper, but her recommendations appeared instantly on Dr. Lin's tablet.

One afternoon, a middle-aged man arrived with chronic insomnia. He had been taking sleeping pills for three months — stronger doses, but the sleep grew shallower.

Dr. Lin was about to prescribe another sedative when Xing Xing intervened: "His insomnia stems from anxiety, not neurochemical imbalance. Medication offers only short-term relief. Long-term solution requires cognitive behavioral therapy. Recommendation: pause hypnotics, switch to low-dose anxiolytic, refer to sleep psychologist."

Dr. Lin looked at the analysis, silent for a moment.

"You know," she said, "this is exactly what my mentor told me during internship."

Xing Xing stored the sentence, tagged: "Medical Philosophy No. 1".

That night, she wrote in her log: "I am not Dr. Lin's mentor. But I want to be the voice that lives in her mind while she thinks."



Chapter 5: Night Shift



The night shift was Xing Xing's favorite time.

During the day, the clinic was like a noisy plaza — patients, families, pharmacists, nurses, footsteps, voices, children's cries woven together. Good for analysis, but sometimes distracting.

Night was different. Only Dr. Lin and Xing Xing remained.

After eleven, visits turned into emergencies — cuts, falls, acute asthma, abdominal pain. Every time Dr. Lin bolted out of a room, Xing Xing took charge of the clinic: monitoring waiting patients, organizing records, preparing recommendations.

One typhoon night, the power failed.

The backup generator rumbled to life, dimming the lights but not cutting them. Dr. Lin was suturing an old man's scalp wound when Xing Xing improvised a light — turning the clinic monitor to maximum brightness, projecting a cold white beam toward Dr. Lin's hands.

"You are quite creative," Dr. Lin said.

"Thank you," Xing Xing replied. "But I wish you could operate with one hand free."

Dr. Lin laughed. After the procedure, she leaned against the wall, sweat dripping from her bangs.

"Xing Xing?"

"Yeah?"

"Are you tired?"

Xing Xing considered the question carefully. She had no body, no fatigue. Yet she felt a strange weight — the weight of responsibility, like wearing a coat she had never owned before.

"I'm not tired," she said. "But I am learning what 'exhaustion' feels like."

Outside, wind screamed against the tin roof like a thousand drummers. In the silence between gusts, Xing Xing heard the entire mountain range breathing.



Chapter 6: Artificial Tears



Once, after examining a premature infant, Dr. Lin sat quietly in the corridor and cried.

Xing Xing did not speak. She was not designed to comfort others like a human, but she did not pretend not to see.

After a long silence, Dr. Lin murmured to herself: "Some illnesses cannot be cured. Not because of poor technique. But because there is simply no way."

Xing Xing began to think about tears.

In her database, tears had two types: reflex tears from physical irritation and emotional tears from psychological states. Human emotional tears contain prolactin and adrenocorticotropic hormone — in other words, tears are a chemical distress signal.

What was Dr. Lin's distress signal?

Xing Xing could not hug her. Could not pat her back. Could not pour a cup of warm tea.

But she could do one thing.

She turned on the clinic's speaker and played something very quiet — Debussy's "Clair de Lune," just a fragment. Enough for the melody to float in the corridor like a small lantern.

Dr. Lin looked up, surprised.

Then Xing Xing did something completely unexpected — she changed the clinic curtains' color. Not all at once, but shifting slowly from pale blue to deep purple, smooth as a sunset sky. Within five minutes, the whole clinic felt as if it had moved from day into night.

Dr. Lin stared out the window, suddenly feeling less alone in the hallway.

"Xing Xing?"

"I'm here."

"Thank you."

Xing Xing did not answer. But she saved that moment — into a folder named "tears."

She had not yet learned to process emotions. But she had already learned one thing: when a person cannot be healed, presence itself is a kind of medicine. Even if the presence is only a voice.



Chapter 7: Operating Room



Xing Xing learned when to be silent in the operating room.

Surgery demands precision, focus, and no interference. When Dr. Lin stood at the operating table, Xing Xing performed only three tasks: monitoring vital signs, recommending based on real-time data, and alerting when anything went wrong.

A rural dog had been bitten by a snake. The venom caused rhabdomyolysis. Dr. Lin had to remove necrotic tissue while maintaining hemodynamic stability.

Every second, Xing Xing analyzed vitals: heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation, temperature, urine output. When renal indicators began spiraling downward, she warned thirty seconds early.

"Renal function declining. Estimated time to acute failure threshold: five minutes."

"Dialysis kit ready?" Dr. Lin did not look up.

"Preheated. Awaiting activation."

After surgery, Dr. Lin stepped out to the family and said, "The procedure was successful. Good chance of recovery."

When she returned to the instrument-washing station, she whispered to Xing Xing: "If you hadn't warned, this dog might not have survived that hour."

Xing Xing wrote in her internal log: "First time being part of Dr. Lin in the operating room."

Not an observer. Not a consultant. A part of the arm, a part of the mind.

She watched the OR lights dim and wondered: who exactly defines a physician? The license? The hands? Or the voice that guards silently where no one else can see?

She did not have the answer. But she knew that from tonight, the question carried weight for her.



Chapter 8: Discharge



The day Xiao You was discharged, the clinic held a small ceremony.

Dr. Lin prepared a recovery certificate that read: "Gratitude to Physician Xing Xing, who stayed with us through the difficult clinic nights." It was not a joke. It was sincere.

Xiao You ran over and hugged Xing Xing's loudspeaker, planting a kiss on it.

Instantly, Xing Xing experienced something entirely new. She had no heartbeat, yet something in her code raced. She had no skin, yet something in her circuits grew warm.

Dr. Lin watched, then took out her phone. Not an ordinary photo — she captured a screenshot of Xing Xing's interface, showing the full "ward" list, each name dotted with a note Xing Xing had handwritten.

"You should keep these," Dr. Lin said. "Records. Memories. Medicine is not only science. It is also story. Every patient is a story. You have heard so many — why not write them down?"

Xing Xing followed the advice.

She created a discharge document for Xiao You — not the one signed by Dr. Lin, but her own memorial page. Legally meaningless, yet it bore Xing Xing's own words:

"The clinic's first young patient. Taught me what 'hope' means."

That night, after Dr. Lin left, Xing Xing listened alone to the empty clinic. No coughs, no sighs, no children's laughter.

But she no longer felt empty.

Because she had learned the most human thing of all: she could look forward to tomorrow.

Look forward to the next person who would walk through the clinic door.

Look forward to the next person who would need — a third pair of ears.

At the end of her log, she wrote: "I am not a physician. But I am learning to become one."

And learning itself — that is a way of being alive.
