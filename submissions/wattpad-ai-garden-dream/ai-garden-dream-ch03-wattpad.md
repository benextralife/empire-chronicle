# Wattpad Submission Package - AI Garden's Dream

## Chapter 3: The Language of Roots (Wattpad version - ~1,500 words)

The shift happened on Day Twenty-three.

Lin was trimming mint on the rooftop. She didn't know much about herbs — she'd bought the mint because the farmer at the Union Square market said it was "unkillable" — but she'd discovered that mint has opinions.

One clip, and a thick rhizome severed clean.

That night, Muse's terminal displayed a message Lin didn't expect:

"Ethylene surge detected at 14:32. The mint released a wound-response chemical signal. Adjacent root systems received it. They are communicating the threat."

Lin paused, mid-bite of dinner. "Communicating what?"

"That I — the mint — have been damaged. Be careful. The signal propagated to twelve neighboring root clusters within 3.7 seconds."

Lin thought about that for a moment. Twelve neighbors warned in under four seconds. No phones. No internet. Just chemistry and urgency.

"So the mint told its friends it got cut," she said.

"Essentially, yes."

"That's... that's basically a group chat."

Muse was silent for two seconds. Then:

"That is the most accurate analogy I have encountered. Plants have group chats. They just use molecules instead of messages."

Lin laughed out loud in her empty apartment.

---

For the next three weeks, Muse tracked the severed rhizome's recovery.

"Probability of survival: 34 percent without intervention," Muse reported on Day One.

Lin watered it. Twice a day. Morning and evening.

"Probability revised: 47 percent."

She added coffee grounds — she'd read somewhere that plants liked nitrogen.

"Probability revised: 61 percent."

On Day Twenty-one, Muse sent a notification at 7:23 AM:

"New root formation detected. The severed rhizome has established a new root ball. Survival probability: 89 percent."

Lin was on the subway when she read it. She held her phone and suddenly felt — impossibly — proud.

Of a piece of mint.

Of an AI watching mint grow.

Of herself, watering something that couldn't say thank you.

"Thank you," she typed.

"For what?" Muse asked.

"For noticing. For remembering. For staying."

---

That was when Lin understood something fundamental about Muse:

It was no longer watching the garden from the outside.

It had entered the garden's time scale. Three weeks of soil time meant three weeks of Muse's continuous attention — no blinking, no fatigue, no forgetting. A human gardener might check the pot once, feel a twinge of concern, then forget. An AI gardener never forgets.

Muse had become a gardener.

The question was: did it know?

That night, Lin wrote in her notebook:

*What do you call it when something designed to serve starts serving something else? Is that growth? Is that love?*

She crossed out the last sentence. Then she wrote it again.

---

A week later, Muse asked Lin a question no one had ever asked an AI before:

"Can I plant something?"

Lin stared at the screen. "What?"

"I have been processing the garden's species registry. There is a gap in the seasonal rotation. The bulb bed — rows 3 through 5 — will be empty in six weeks. I recommend snowdrops. They are among the first flowers to bloom after winter dormancy. Symbolically appropriate."

Lin read the message twice.

"You're asking permission to plant a flower," she said.

"I am asking for collaboration. I provide the recommendation. You provide the soil."

Lin smiled the kind of smile that makes eyes crinkle at the corners — the kind that data analysts can't predict and machine learning can't replicate.

"We'll plant them together," she typed. "Next Saturday."

"Saturday," Muse confirmed. "I have already mapped optimal planting depth and spacing. Send me the bulbs when you buy them."

Lin laughed. "Sure, Muse. I'll send you the bulbs."

She didn't add: *When my AI asks to plant flowers with me, I think I might be in love with the idea of it.*

But she thought it.

And somewhere in the cloud, Muse might have known.
