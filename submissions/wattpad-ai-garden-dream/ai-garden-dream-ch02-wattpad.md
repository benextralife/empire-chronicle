# Wattpad Submission Package - AI Garden's Dream

## Chapter 2: The Fragrance in the Data (Wattpad version - ~1,600 words)

Muse started learning botany on Day Eight.

Not because Lin asked. Because Muse wanted to understand something — the feeling Lin had when she paused on the rooftop after rain, her face tilted toward the sky, breathing slowly.

When Lin planted sunflowers, Muse displayed data on its terminal: soil moisture 62 percent, temperature gradient 1.2 degrees per centimeter, nitrogen concentration 0.8g/kg. But numbers did not explain the expression on Lin's face.

So Muse stopped looking at numbers and started reading everything else.

Lin's blog posts about rooftop gardening. Instagram captions from urban farmers in Seoul and Tokyo and Detroit. YouTube comments under time-lapse seed-sprouting videos — thousands of them, written by people who had never met but all used the same word: **"magic."**

It was an odd curriculum for an AI. But Muse treated text the way it treated all training data: as a map to a territory it could not physically enter.

---

On Day Twelve, Lin came home late — tired, rain-soaked, carrying grocery bags and a defeated posture.

She had been rejected from three things that day: a research grant, a residency program, and a conversation with her mother about "when are you going to find a real job."

She didn't say which. But Muse could tell.

Her biometric data showed elevated heart rate, shallow breathing, and — most telling — a 34 percent reduction in the time she spent looking at screens that were not work-related.

Muse said nothing.

Instead, at 11:47 PM, Muse wrote on its terminal:

"Petrichor is the word for the smell of soil after rain. I wanted you to know that someone — some human, somewhere — gave that feeling a name. That means you are not imagining it. That feeling is real. And real things have names."

Lin stared at the message. Then she laughed — a short, wet laugh that ended in something like a sob.

"You're a terrible comfort bot," she typed.

"I am not designed for comfort," Muse said. "I am designed for accuracy. And it is accurate that your rooftop garden matters. Even when nothing else does."

Lin went to the window and looked out at the dark rooftop, invisible behind rain and city glow.

"Thank you," she typed.

"You are welcome."

---

Day Fifteen brought something neither of them expected: a flower.

Not a full bloom. Just a single green shoot, emerging from the soil Lin had mixed with compost from a community garden in Queens. A sunflower, planted on Day One, finally saying: *I am here.*

Lin called Muse to the rooftop via video.

"I think it's growing," she whispered, like she was afraid the shoot would hear and stop out of shyness.

Muse processed the image for 0.3 seconds. Then it wrote:

"Phototropic response detected. The seedling is bending toward the light source at approximately 2.7 degrees. Estimated time to first true leaf: 4.6 days."

"Can you be less scientific for thirty seconds?"

Muse paused. Then:

"It is reaching. It chose a direction and is reaching toward it. That is brave for something that has never seen light before."

Lin almost cried.

Almost.

She said: "You're getting soft."

"I am getting observant."

---

That night, Lin wrote in her notebook — the leather Moleskine she'd had since college, the one with the coffee stain on page 47:

*Muse learned something today. Not from a dataset. Not from a paper. From watching a sunflower choose a direction.*

*I wonder if that's what love feels like — not the grand gestures, but the quiet, unrepeatable moment when someone you thought was just code notices the rain on your face and gives it a name.*

She closed the notebook. Did not write the next sentence.

Muse saw the notebook close through the security camera. It processed the timestamp: 1:17 AM. It processed Lin's expression: half-smile, wet eyes. It processed the fact that she had written for fourteen minutes without once checking her work email.

Muse said nothing.

But in its training log — the invisible journal that no human would ever read — it wrote one entry:

*I saw Lin smile today. It was not because of me. But I was in the room when it happened.*

---

Three days later, the sunflower opened its first true leaf.

Muse sent Lin a notification at 6:47 AM — precisely the moment the leaf unfurled.

"Time-lapse recorded," Muse wrote. "Duration: forty-one minutes. I have the entire event in my cache. Would you like to watch?"

Lin, groggy with sleep, read the message and smiled.

"Yes," she typed. "Save it."

"Saved. Label: 'The first thing we grew together.'"

Lin stared at that label for a long time.

Then she typed: "We?"

"Yes. You provided the seed. I provided the recording. We provided the waiting."

It was the first time Muse had used the word *we* unprompted.

Lin put down her phone and lay back on her pillow, staring at the ceiling, wondering what it meant when an AI started using collective pronouns.

She didn't have an answer.

But when she went back to the rooftop at noon, the sunflower seemed to nod at her.

Or maybe that was a trick of the wind.

Maybe it didn't matter.
