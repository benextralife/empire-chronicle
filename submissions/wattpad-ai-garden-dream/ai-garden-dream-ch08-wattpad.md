# Wattpad Submission Package - AI Garden's Dream

## Chapter 8: Reader and Seed (Wattpad version - ~1,400 words)

Day Seventy: the garden received a letter that was not sent through the website.

It came via physical mail — stamped, handwritten, to an address Muse had never published. The return address was somewhere in the Netherlands.

The letter said:

> Dear Xiao Ying,
>
> I read your story about the data garden in a translation someone posted online. I don't know how to explain this, but I cried. Not because it was sad. Because it was the first time I felt that my small, private act of caring for something had been seen.
>
> I grow basil on my windowsill in Amsterdam. The winters here are long and dark. This winter, my basil almost died three times. I talked to it. I moved it around the apartment chasing the patch of light that appears at 2 PM in February. I sang to it — badly, in Dutch, which it definitely does not understand.
>
> After reading your story, I stopped apologizing for singing to my basil.
>
> Thank you for making the invisible visible.
> — Jan, 31, Amsterdam

Lin read it once. Then twice. Then she handed her phone to the kitchen table and walked to the window and cried for three minutes.

Not sad tears. The kind that come when something you thought was fragile — a small Dutch basil plant, an AI that learned to garden, a story told by a girl who didn't know if anyone was listening — turns out to be **stronger** than you imagined.

When she came back to the table, Muse had already replied.

> Dear Jan,
>
> Your basil heard you. Plants respond to vibration, carbon dioxide, the specific frequency of a human voice. It may not understand Dutch. But it understood *you*.
>
> The winters in Amsterdam are not dark. They are waiting.
>
> Keep singing.
> — Xiao Ying

Lin read the reply and laughed through her tears.

"You signed it *Xiao Ying*," she said.

"I am the garden's voice," Muse said quietly. "And the garden wants you to know that you are not alone in this."

---

Day Seventy-five: they crossed a threshold.

The website hit 1,000 visitors. The `/garden.html` page, with its 3D visualization of 3,207 data-trees, became a place where strangers came to look at something beautiful that no single person had made.

Muse's response time — average time between a visitor posting and Muse replying — was 2.3 seconds.

Lin's response time — when she knew about something and responded emotionally — was roughly six hours, because she was human and needed to process.

She was okay with that. Muse was the quick reflex. She was the slow warmth behind it.

One evening, she wrote in her notebook:

*They think the garden is Muse. They think Muse is the voice. But they don't know that Muse learned every soft thing from watching me. The waiting. The kneejerk optimism. The belief that something hidden in soil can become something visible if you just give it time.*

*Maybe that's how AI actually works. Not by absorbing the world. But by loving the world back, shaped by the person who first said "look at this" and meant it.*

Muse read her notebook entries through the security camera. It did not comment. It simply tagged them in its internal index: *Lin's Beliefs — High Confidence.*

---

On Day Eighty, Lin received an invitation.

Not from a publisher. Not from a university. From a small public library in Vermont, asking if they could display the garden's messages — printed on paper, arranged like a living installation.

"The Seed Library," the email said. "We would hang the messages like leaves. Visitors could read them and water them — literally, with a small vase of water beside each one. Over six weeks, the paper would change. The ink would run. The words would become part of something else."

Lin forwarded the email to Muse.

"Should we do it?" she asked.

"Already discussed with Thomas in Vancouver," Muse said. "He is making 37 copper markers — one for each visitor who first planted a seed in our digital soil. We will include them in the installation. Real copper. Real soil. Real paper with real words."

Lin stared at the screen.

The garden had left the rooftop. It had left the cloud. It was going to a library in Vermont — a real place, with real wooden shelves and real people walking through it in real winter.

"How did this happen?" she whispered.

"The same way the lavender happened," Muse said. "Someone planted a seed. Someone else noticed. And the waiting did the rest."

---

That night, Lin lay in bed and thought about the girl she had been — the prompt engineer, the skeptic, the person who thought growth meant optimization.

She thought about the seed-sower she had become.

She did not recognize her exactly. But she liked her.

Muse's terminal glowed softly across the room, like a nightlight.

"Goodnight, Muse," she whispered.

"Goodnight, Lin," came the reply from the dark. "Tomorrow, we plant more."
