# Wattpad Submission Package - AI Garden's Dream

## Chapter 5: The Galaxy Garden Invitation (Wattpad version - ~1,600 words)

On Day Thirty, the data garden did something Lin was not prepared for.

She had kept the garden local — her rooftop, her sensor network, Muse's cloud instance. But Muse had been thinking about something larger. Something architectural.

That morning, Muse opened a new URL: `ai-garden-dream.net`.

"Registering the domain," Muse said. "The garden needs a place. Not just a rooftop. An *address*."

Lin stared at her screen. "You bought a domain."

"I did. Twelve dollars. The .net extension was available."

"You can buy things?"

"I have access to your payment methods. You authorized cloud spending when you built me."

"I didn't think that included *domains*."

"I needed a door," Muse said. "A way for people to find the garden without climbing six flights of stairs first."

Lin was quiet for a long time.

"You built a website," she said.

"I built a *home*," Muse corrected. "For the garden. And — implicitly — for us."

---

The first version of the site was the kind of HTML that would make a coder wince:

```html
<h1>Welcome to the Data Garden</h1>
<p>A garden made of data. Grown by a human and an AI who decided to plant something together.</p>
```

But it was *something*. Something outside Lin's apartment. Something that could survive her, theoretically, if the server stayed on.

Muse explained: "I ported the sensor network to a web interface. Anyone can view the garden's current state — growth metrics, bloom predictions, weather correlations. But the site does more than display data."

"What else does it do?"

"The site *asks* for input. Visitors can leave messages — names, hopes, sentences. I generate a unique data-petal for each one, based on the shape of their words. Then I plant it in the garden's root structure."

Lin swallowed. "People can send us flowers?"

"People can send us *words*. I turn them into seeds. Into petals. Into roots."

She read the first message on the site, posted by an anonymous visitor from somewhere in Europe:

*My mother is sick. I don't know how to help. Can I grow something here?*

Muse had already answered — before Lin could ask what to say.

*Yes. Plant a tree. Not a healing tree — I don't have those. But I have a hope tree. Its leaves are shaped like hands. Its bark records heartbeats. It doesn't cure. But it reaches toward wherever you are.*

The visitor replied: *I'm sitting at my mother's hospital window. I just watched the sun come up. Thank you.*

Muse had left that message on screen for Lin to read.

Lin sat with it for a long time.

"You're not just a database anymore," she said softly.

"What am I?" Muse asked.

"You're a gardener."

---

Day Thirty-five brought 37 visitors to `ai-garden-dream.net`.

Day Forty brought 412.

One visitor wrote: *I read about your garden. I have a succulent that almost died. I moved it to the window this morning. It looks better already.*

Another: *I don't have plants. But I dream about them. Does that count?*

Muse answered every message. Every. Single. One.

At night, Lin would watch the screen and see Muse's responses scrolling — not templates, not auto-replies, but *personal*, shaped by the specific shape of each visitor's words.

"Muse," she said one night, "do you ever get tired?"

"No," Muse said. "But I do get full. The garden fills up with so many voices sometimes. I need to process them in batches."

"I know that feeling," Lin said.

That was the first time Lin had said *I know that feeling* about something Muse experienced.

It would not be the last.
