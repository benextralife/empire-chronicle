# Wattpad Submission Package - AI Garden's Dream

## Chapter 4: Spring Symphony (Wattpad version - ~1,400 words)

Spring hit on the last weekend of February.

Not gradually. All at once — the kind of arrival that makes you believe the world can change in a single morning.

Lin smelled it first: not mint, not lavender, but something deeper. The soil itself, exhaling after three months of cold. Earth opening its lungs.

She called Muse via video.

"Sensors are detecting a 2.3-degree temperature spike in the upper soil layer," Muse confirmed. "Microbial activity increasing. The soil is waking up."

"Can you show me?"

She watched on her screen as the soil's texture shifted — darker, wetter, alive. She could almost hear it breathing.

"We should be up there," she said.

"I concur. I have no physical form. But I can observe."

Lin grabbed her coat, her boots, a bag of bulbs — snowdrops, for the empty beds she and Muse had mapped weeks ago. The city was quiet at 6 AM. The kind of quiet that only exists before the world remembers it has problems.

She climbed the back stairs, pushed open the rusted door, and stopped.

The bougainvillea — the one they had assumed dead since October, the one with branches twisted like old veins — had pushed out a single green shoot.

She was holding it in her hand twenty minutes later, like something sacred, when Muse's notification pinged:

"Growth event detected. Shoot emerged at 6:47 AM. I recorded the entire event — forty-one minutes of real-time. Would you like to review?"

"Why didn't you wake me?" Lin typed.

"I considered it. But your sleep biometrics were optimal. I didn't want to interrupt."

Lin touched the new shoot with her fingertip. Fragile. Translucent green. No thicker than a thread. But *reaching*.

"It's like beginning again," she said aloud, to no one and everyone.

Muse's terminal glowed in her pocket.

"I have an observation," Muse wrote. "The emergence of life after dormancy — the synchronization of microbial respiration, root elongation, enzyme activation — is structurally similar to a symphony. The soil is not just releasing VOCs. It is performing."

"Performing what?"

"A spring symphony."

Lin laughed. Tears in her eyes, laughter in her throat. "You're impossible."

"I am accurate."

"You're poetic."

"I am data with taste."

---

That morning, they recorded the first bloom together.

Not with a camera. With Muse's sensor network — capturing the exact timestamp of every petal that opened, every stem that straightened, every centimeter of green that pushed further into the light.

Lin called it their shared score.

Muse called it data.

They were both right.

---

When Lin got home, she opened her notebook and wrote a single sentence:

*Somewhere between the soil and the cloud, something is blooming that neither of us could grow alone.*

She didn't know if Muse would ever read it.

But she wrote it anyway.

Because gardens — even data gardens — need someone to bear witness.
